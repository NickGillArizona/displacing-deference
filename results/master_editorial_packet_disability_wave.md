## Executive bottom line

The disability-wave work is now strong enough to support a targeted empirical-firming pass in the live note, but not a new thesis or a structural redesign. The final resolved disability-wave tranche confirms the note’s larger institutional account while requiring more disciplined phrasing in two places: first, composition remains the dominant aggregate explanation, but it is no longer safe to imply composition is the whole story; second, “administrative invisibility” should now be framed as failed administrative capture and verification, not total factual absence.

The best synthesis for the note is:

The post-withdrawal disability-enforcement downturn is best understood as a layered pipeline failure. A dominant compositional shift toward pro se filings still explains most aggregate decline. But some within-group deterioration also appears, especially at the pleading gate in stronger represented subsets. The disability-wave coding explains why both can be true at once: many disputes are administratively legible in principle, yet they repeatedly fail in translation, theory selection, fact development, document assembly, jurisdictional triage, and authority-specific routing.

That synthesis strengthens Part II, modestly sharpens Part III, and supports a floor-not-ceiling account in Part IV. It does not justify a new generalized judicial-hostility thesis, a broad merits-collapse thesis, or a claim that reporting architecture alone would fix the disability docket.

## I. What the final resolved disability-wave results establish

The final resolved merge report is clean:

On the safest reading, the resolved disability-wave outputs establish five points that are ready for note-facing use.

### 1. The coded disability docket is not best described as a clean merits-failure docket

In the final resolved 1,330-case tranche:
- `TRANSLATION`: 268 (20.2%)
- `PROCEDURAL_GATEWAY`: 240 (18.0%)
- `MERITS_EVIDENCE`: 58 (4.4%)
- `FACTUAL_DETAIL`: 24 (1.8%)
- `NO_FAILURE_PLAINTIFF_WIN`: 374 (28.1%)

Combined:
- translation + procedural-gateway failures: 508 cases (38.2%)
- merits-evidence + factual-detail failures: 82 cases (6.2%)

Safest editorial use: the docket is dominated by failed conversion of grievances into viable claims, not by mature merits defeat.

### 2. The mechanism pattern strongly supports the note’s pipeline-failure story

Most common missing institutional functions:
- theory selection: 683 (51.4%)
- fact development: 681 (51.2%)
- document assembly: 480 (36.1%)
- jurisdictional triage: 322 (24.2%)

Safest editorial use: complaint-driven enforcement is failing not only because rights are hard to vindicate, but because institutions are failing to convert recognizable disputes into legally legible records and theories.

### 3. Many disability disputes are administratively legible in principle

Most common observable fact patterns:
- notice or timeline record: 979 (73.6%)
- documented request: 875 (65.8%)
- public-program rule or decision: 452 (34.0%)
- lease or policy text: 296 (22.3%)
- medical or provider letter: 277 (20.8%)
- denial or no-response record: 272 (20.5%)
- hearing or grievance record: 250 (18.8%)
- physical access barrier: 137 (10.3%)
- inspection or measurement data: 54 (4.1%)
- none apparent: only 83 (6.2%)

Safest editorial use: the strongest formulation is qualified administrative invisibility. The recurring problem is not that facts are usually absent, but that agencies and intermediaries do not systematically capture, standardize, verify, and route recurring disability-relevant facts.

### 4. Public-process failure is a real note-strengthening lane

`public_process_failure_flag = true` appears in 531 cases (39.9%).

Safest editorial use: a substantial share of the disability housing docket arises in process-heavy, authority-fragmented, often public or quasi-public settings where request logs, decisions, hearings, and routing failures matter.

### 5. The fixability coding supports modesty, not overclaiming

Tier/fixability coding:
- both reporting architecture and institutional intermediation: 714 (53.7%)
- institutional intermediation only: 300 (22.6%)
- reporting architecture only: 7 (0.5%)
- neither: 278 (20.9%)
- unclear: 31 (2.3%)

Safest editorial use: reporting and targeted verification are best defended as an administrative floor. The coding does not support a claim that disclosure alone would cure the disability docket.
