# Pro Se Pleading-Mechanism Divergence Memo

This memo synthesizes the existing overnight outputs into a note-facing claim: the disability FHA docket is not just generally plaintiff-hostile. It is filtered through a distinct pro se pleading mechanism in which procedural authorities, screening rules, and translation failures do disproportionate work against uncounseled plaintiffs, while represented cases continue to reach and sometimes win on substantive FHA grounds.

## Bottom line

Three existing-output facts line up unusually well:

1. The citation baseline is dominated by pleading authorities, especially `Ashcroft v. Iqbal` and `Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly`.
2. That procedural dominance is much more concentrated in the pro se subset than in the represented subset.
3. The broader outcomes memos show that the apparent collapse in disability-case performance is driven mostly by composition and gatekeeping at the pleading stage, not by a comparable collapse in represented merits performance.

That combination supports a sharper note claim: the modern disability docket is being thinned before doctrine develops, and the thinning mechanism is disproportionately pro se.

- Represented Twombly:Cleburne ratio = 293:73, or about 4.0:1.

That is not just a difference in volume. It suggests a difference in what the opinions are doing.

## What the pro se mechanism currently looks like

The current-data disability comparison report gives the cleanest existing map of pro se pleading-loss mechanisms.

|---|---:|---:|

| SCREENING_1915 | 164 | 10.7% |
| JURISDICTIONAL | 163 | 10.6% |
| STATUTE_OF_LIMITATIONS | 49 | 3.2% |
| NEXUS_FAILURE | 44 | 2.9% |
| STANDING | 20 | 1.3% |
| Other listed reasons combined | 19 | 1.2% |

Two points matter here.

First, this is overwhelmingly an Iqbal/Twombly story at the current descriptive level: about seven in ten pro se disability pleading-loss claim rows are coded that way.

Second, the next two biggest buckets are not substantive FHA merits defeats. They are process filters: `SCREENING_1915` and `JURISDICTIONAL`.

So the dominant mechanism is not “courts reject disability accommodation law on the merits.” It is “claims are being screened out before substantive law does much work.”

## Why this matters for doctrine, not just outcomes

The note should not stop at “pro se plaintiffs lose more.” The better claim is that pro se filtering helps explain doctrinal underdevelopment.

The existing outputs already support that move.

1. `unified_overnight_citation_baseline.md` shows the top two authorities by a massive margin are Iqbal and Twombly.
2. `unified_follow_on_opportunities.md` shows those authorities appear far more often in pro se records than represented records.
3. `MEMO_H7_H1_H2_FINDINGS.md` shows represented merits performance is relatively stable, while pleading-stage terminations are rising and now account for the largest share of outcomes.
4. The same H1 memo shows open-textured claims are nearly nonviable: broad win rate 1.0%, and 88.5% of open-textured cases are pro se.

That last point is especially important for the note’s AFFH-facing ambition. If open-textured or structurally framed disability claims are disproportionately pro se and almost never survive, then the docket is not only sorting winners from losers. It is sorting which kinds of FHA doctrine ever get elaborated in published opinions.

In other words: the pleading mechanism is also a doctrine-production mechanism.

## Best note-facing synthesis

The strongest bounded claim from the current record is:

- The disability FHA docket is procedurally saturated.
- That procedural saturation is disproportionately concentrated in pro se cases.
- The concentration takes recognizable forms: Iqbal/Twombly pleading failure first, with § 1915 screening and threshold defects next.
- Because represented cases continue to survive MTD at high rates, the headline deterioration in disability outcomes is better understood as a failure of access, translation, and case formation than as a uniform collapse in substantive FHA law.
- That access failure has doctrinal consequences: it suppresses the set of disability and AFFH-adjacent theories that ever receive mature substantive treatment.

## Suggested use in the note

This memo can support a short section making four moves:

1. Open with the citation baseline: the screened disability corpus is dominated by Iqbal and Twombly.
2. Show the divergence: those pleading authorities appear in 56.1% of pro se citation-bearing records but only 29.1% of represented ones.
3. Link the divergence to outcomes: represented MTD survival remains about 82%, while pro se survival is around 20-29% depending on period.
4. Convert the descriptive gap into a doctrinal point: pro se pleading failure is not merely an equity problem; it is one reason disability FHA doctrine, including more structural and implementation-centered theories, remains underdeveloped.

## Framing language the note can plausibly use

Possible note-facing formulation:

“The disability docket’s modern harshness is not exhausted by aggregate plaintiff loss. Existing case-level and citation-level outputs suggest a more specific mechanism: uncounseled disability claims are disproportionately routed through procedural gatekeeping, especially Iqbal/Twombly dismissal and related screening devices, while represented cases still reach substantive FHA doctrine at much higher rates. The result is not only fewer plaintiff wins, but fewer occasions for courts to articulate disability-centered housing doctrine at all.”

## Limits

Two limits should be stated clearly.

1. The `unified_overnight_disability_comparison_report.md` and `unified_overnight_temporal_intensification.md` memos are current-data approximations rather than the richer refined autopsy layer.
2. The present memo identifies a strong mechanism pattern, but the next refinement step would still be close reading of the already-mapped ambiguous pleading-loss cases to separate boilerplate citation from citation doing real doctrinal work.

Even with those limits, the existing overnight outputs are already strong enough to support the note’s central intermediate claim: the disability docket is being shaped by a pro se pleading filter that both depresses outcomes and constrains doctrinal development.
