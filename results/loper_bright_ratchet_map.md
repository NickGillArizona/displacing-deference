# The Loper Bright Ratchet: A Doctrinal Map of How *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* Asymmetrically Affects HUD's Statutory Authority to Collect and Report Disability-Disaggregated Housing Data

**Author (memo):** Research memo for law review note author
**Date:** April 16, 2026
**Target article:** *Displacing Deference: Data and Doctrine for a Disability-Centered AFFH*
**Cite:** Loper Bright Enters. v. Raimondo, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024)

---

## I. Executive Summary

**Headline finding.** *Loper Bright* helps the Note's statutory-authority argument more than it hurts it. The ratchet runs in a single direction for one simple reason: HUD has never issued a binding interpretation that *limits* the reach of 42 U.S.C. § 3608(e)(6) or 42 U.S.C. § 3614a. What Chevron deference used to protect was HUD's *inaction* — the agency's practical decision, sustained across thirty-seven years, to operationalize only race/ethnicity fields on Form HUD-27061 despite a facially mandatory statutory command to report "data on the . . . handicap . . . of persons" in federally assisted housing. Post-*Loper*, that inaction is no longer insulated. Courts must read § 3608(e)(6) and § 3614a de novo. And on the text, the Note's position is the stronger one.

**One-paragraph ratchet thesis.** The shift from Chevron to *Loper Bright* is asymmetric because pre-*Loper* deference protected both agency *action* (plausible agency interpretations that could block the Note's reading) and agency *inaction* (HUD's failure to promulgate or operationalize Part 121's disability fields, which courts could frame as a reasonable non-exercise of discretion). Post-*Loper*, only one of those bulwarks collapses for HUD: the agency still owes no deference to a court reading § 3608(e)(6)'s "shall . . . annually report . . . data on the . . . handicap" as a textual mandate. Meanwhile, § 3614a's unusually specific rulemaking grant — which expressly authorizes "rules for the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data . . . to carry out this subchapter" — is the kind of textually explicit delegation that *Loper Bright* preserves, not the kind it destabilizes. In short: *Loper Bright* strips the deference shield from HUD's inaction while leaving HUD's textual data-collection authority intact. The Note's framing — that HUD's thirty-seven-year omission of disability from Form HUD-27061 is a textual violation, not a discretionary policy choice — is the precise argument *Loper Bright* empowers.

## II. Statutory Architecture Map: The FHA's Data / Disclosure / Recordkeeping Skeleton

The Fair Housing Act's data-authority architecture rests on five interlocking provisions. Exact text (verified against Cornell LII and the U.S. Code) follows.

### A. 42 U.S.C. § 3608(a) — The Administrative Anchor

> "The authority and responsibility for administering this Act shall be in the Secretary of Housing and Urban Development."

*Textual move useful to the Note:* "authority and responsibility" uses conjoined nouns — the Secretary is not merely empowered but *obligated*. This is not a discretionary grant; it is a duty grant. Under *Loper*, "shall" language with duty-bearing nouns reads as mandatory absent contrary text.

### B. 42 U.S.C. § 3608(e)(5) — The AFFH Hook

> The Secretary shall "administer the programs and activities relating to housing and urban development in a manner affirmatively to further the policies of this subchapter."

*Textual move:* The AFFH duty is *programmatic* — it attaches to all programs and activities. Post-*Loper*, the duty cannot be read as metaphorical; "administer . . . in a manner affirmatively to further" requires affirmative administrative conduct, which necessarily presupposes data sufficient to measure whether administration is "furthering" the policies.

### C. 42 U.S.C. § 3608(e)(6) — THE TEXTUAL ANCHOR

> The Secretary shall "annually report to the Congress, and make available to the public, data on the race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, handicap, and family characteristics of persons and households who are applicants for, participants in, or beneficiaries of programs administered by the Department."

*Textual moves useful to the Note:*

1. **"Shall . . . annually report"** — mandatory verb; recurring temporal obligation.
2. **"Data on the race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, handicap, and family characteristics"** — the enumerated characteristics are connected by commas and an "and"; "handicap" sits structurally identical to "race." There is no textual hierarchy; a reading that disability may be omitted while race must be collected is textually untenable absent an express carve-out (and § 3608(f), which scopes the statutes triggering the reporting duty, contains none for disability).
3. **"Data on the . . . handicap . . . of persons"** — a reporting obligation logically presupposes a collection obligation; the Secretary cannot "report data on handicap" she does not have.
4. **"Make available to the public"** — the disclosure duty runs to the public, not just to Congress; it is not satisfiable through executive-branch internal correspondence.

### D. 42 U.S.C. § 3614a — The Rulemaking / Data-Rule Grant

> "The Secretary may make rules (including rules for the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data) to carry out this subchapter. The Secretary shall give public notice and opportunity for comment with respect to all rules made under this section."

*Textual moves useful to the Note:*

1. **Parenthetical specificity** — Congress did not leave "data collection" to implication. The parenthetical expressly names "the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data" as a species of rulemaking authorized by the provision. This is not a "modest" or "ancillary" authority — Congress wrote it into the text.
2. **"To carry out this subchapter"** — post-*Loper* courts have upheld rulemaking grants framed in materially identical language as "necessary to carry out" an agency's statutory functions. *See infra* Part VII (ITC, IRS, DOL examples). *Loper Bright* itself flagged that express rulemaking delegations — including "prescribe regulations necessary for administration" and analogous formulations — remain robust. 144 S. Ct. at 2263.
3. **APA notice-and-comment requirement** — the second sentence independently shows Congress contemplated binding rulemaking under § 3614a. This cuts against any reading that § 3614a is "merely advisory."

### E. 42 U.S.C. § 3535(d) — The Housekeeping Rulemaking Grant

> "The Secretary may delegate any of his functions, powers, and duties to such officers and employees of the Department as he may designate, may authorize such successive redelegations of such functions, powers, and duties as he may deem desirable, and may make such rules and regulations as may be necessary to carry out his functions, powers, and duties."

*Textual move:* § 3535(d) is a Department-wide housekeeping grant; it supplies belt-and-suspenders rulemaking authority for any *function*, *power*, or *duty* vested in the Secretary, including those located in § 3608. Because the "functions . . . and duties" include the § 3608(e)(6) annual-report duty, the rulemaking authority to operationalize that duty is not merely implied — it is doubly textual (§ 3614a plus § 3535(d)).

### F. 24 C.F.R. Part 121 — The Regulatory Overlay

HUD adopted 24 C.F.R. Part 121 ("Collection of Data") in 1989 pursuant to § 3614a. Part 121 contemplates data collection on the full § 3608(e)(6) roster, including handicap/disability. HUD has never operationalized the disability fields; Form HUD-27061-H ("Race and Ethnic Data Reporting Form") collects only race and ethnicity. HUD's September 2022 Paperwork Reduction Act notice acknowledged the omission and indicated HUD "intends to update this form to collect protected class data as required by the Fair Housing Act and HUD regulations at 24 CFR 121." 87 Fed. Reg. 58,734 (Sept. 27, 2022). That update has not occurred as of the date of this memo.

|---|---|---|---|

| § 3608(e)(5) (AFFH) | HUD's successive interpretations (1994 rule, 2015 rule, 2020 rescission, 2021 interim, 2023 proposed) each received Chevron deference or escaped review as mere guidance. | Court reads "administer . . . in a manner affirmatively to further" de novo. | **HELPS asymmetrically**: courts cannot hide behind HUD's interpretive ping-pong. |

| § 3614a | Chevron deference covered the scope of HUD's rulemaking. | De novo; but the express parenthetical ("rules for the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data") makes this precisely the kind of textually explicit delegation *Loper Bright* preserves. The critical pre/post asymmetry: Chevron's reasonableness standard was **symmetric** — it could be invoked to bless an agency's expansion *or* its contraction of statutory duties. De novo review is **asymmetric** relative to the text. If the text is mandatory, the court reads it that way regardless of agency practice. If the text is permissive, the court likewise reads it that way. Because § 3608(e)(6) is mandatory on its face, the shift to de novo is monotonically helpful to the Note's argument.

## IV. Textual Analysis of § 3608(e)(6) Post-*Loper*

### A. Does "data on . . . handicap" require disability-disaggregated collection?

Yes, on the text. Three independent textual arguments:

1. **Grammatical parity with race.** The Note's opponent would have to explain why "data on race" compels disaggregated collection of racial data (as HUD has done for decades via Form HUD-27061-H) but "data on . . . handicap" does not. There is no textual distinction. Under *Loper*'s de novo posture, the court owes no deference to HUD's implicit view that the enumerated characteristics may be read with a hierarchy. The canon against surplusage, and the presumption of consistent usage within a single clause, both cut against HUD's selective operationalization.

2. **Plain meaning of "data on."** "Data on X" is a phrase whose ordinary meaning presupposes that X is a category on which discrete observations are collected. To "report data on handicap" without collecting disability-disaggregated observations is a category error. Post-*Loper* courts have shown particular willingness to rely on dictionary-grounded ordinary meaning. See, e.g., Tennessee v. EEOC, 128 F.4th 996 (8th Cir. 2025) (panel noting that post-*Loper* review treats regulatory terms as "first, a question of what the text says").

3. **Temporal adverb "annually."** The annual cadence presupposes an ongoing data infrastructure. Congress cannot have meant HUD to assemble reports from data it has not collected.

### B. Does "annually report to Congress" require HUD to HAVE the data?

The implicature is textually inescapable. A reporting duty that depends on a collection predicate cannot be satisfied without the predicate. The structural argument:

- § 3608(e)(6) imposes a duty to report.
- The duty to report is to "annually report . . . data on the . . . handicap."
- HUD cannot report data it does not have.
- Therefore, § 3608(e)(6) imposes, at minimum, an implied duty to collect what it expressly requires HUD to report.

This is not a "major question" under *West Virginia v. EPA*, 597 U.S. 697 (2022), because the scope of the asserted authority — collecting one additional demographic field on an existing form — is modest, is expressly contemplated by 24 C.F.R. Part 121, and is directly traceable to a statutory subsection that already names "handicap."

### C. Does thirty-seven years of omission violate the text?

Yes — and this is precisely where *Loper* is most useful. Pre-*Loper*, the omission could be recharacterized as HUD's reasonable interpretation that "data on . . . handicap" permits aggregate reporting from other sources (e.g., the American Housing Survey). That characterization drew whatever strength it had from Chevron step two reasonableness. Post-*Loper*, no such move is available: there is no formal HUD interpretation to defer to, and the text's plain reading forecloses aggregate-only substitution. The argument sharpens to: HUD has, as a matter of statutory fact, failed to perform a non-discretionary duty Congress imposed in 1988.

### D. The Strongest Textual Argument, Compressed

> Section 3608(e)(6) directs the Secretary, in mandatory language, to "annually report to the Congress, and make available to the public, data on the . . . handicap . . . of persons and households" in federally assisted housing. That duty is non-discretionary on its face. The duty to report presupposes the duty to collect; HUD cannot report data it does not possess. HUD's thirty-seven-year failure to collect disability-disaggregated data on Form HUD-27061, despite adopting 24 C.F.R. Part 121 in 1989 for precisely this purpose, is a textual violation of § 3608(e)(6). Before *Loper Bright*, that violation might have been blunted by Chevron-style deference to HUD's practice. After *Loper Bright*, courts must exercise their independent judgment on the meaning of "shall . . . annually report . . . data on the . . . handicap." The text admits only one reading: the Secretary must collect and report disability-disaggregated data.

## V. Textual Analysis of § 3614a Post-*Loper*

### A. "To carry out this subchapter" — a protected delegation

*Loper Bright* did not disturb Congress's power to delegate. 144 S. Ct. at 2263 ("Congress has often enacted such statutes" that "expressly delegate to an agency the authority to give meaning to a particular statutory term . . . or to fill up the details of a statutory scheme."). Rulemaking grants phrased as "necessary to carry out" have been repeatedly upheld post-*Loper*. See, e.g., Suzlon Energy Ltd. v. United States, No. 23-2141 (Fed. Cir. 2025) (treating ITC's grant under 19 U.S.C. § 1335 to adopt "such reasonable procedures, rules, and regulations as it may deem necessary to carry out its functions" as a clear delegation surviving de novo review); Byers v. Commissioner, No. 24-1460 (8th Cir. 2025) (upholding IRS whistleblower rule as consistent with Code).

### B. The unusual parenthetical is decisive

Section 3614a is not a generic "carry out" grant. The parenthetical — "(including rules for the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data)" — is an express congressional identification of data-collection rulemaking as a permitted exercise. Under *Loper Bright*, the best reading of a statute governs; and the best reading of a grant that names "collection . . . of appropriate data" is that such rulemaking is within the delegated zone. This is the opposite of what happened in *Marin Audubon Society v. FAA*, 121 F.4th 902 (D.C. Cir. 2024): there, the D.C. Circuit held that CEQ lacked rulemaking authority because NEPA nowhere conferred it. Here, FHA § 3614a textually confers it.

### C. Discretion ≠ absence of duty

Section 3614a uses "may" ("The Secretary may make rules"), which, standing alone, suggests discretion. But the relevant question for the Note is not whether HUD *may* issue data rules — it is whether HUD *must* report disability data under § 3608(e)(6). Section 3614a's permissive "may" supplies the *how*; § 3608(e)(6)'s mandatory "shall" supplies the *what*. The combined structure is: Congress made the outcome (reporting disability data) mandatory, and left the form of implementing rules to HUD's expert choice. Post-*Loper*, a court performing de novo review can compel the outcome without second-guessing the form.

### D. The § 3535(d) backup

If a court balks at grounding a compulsion remedy in § 3614a's permissive "may," § 3535(d)'s belt-and-suspenders housekeeping language — "shall . . . make such rules . . . as may be necessary to carry out his functions, powers, and duties" — supplies the missing "shall" on the rulemaking side. The functions and duties include § 3608(e)(6)'s annual-report duty. The chain is: § 3608(e)(6) creates the reporting duty; § 3535(d) creates a duty to promulgate rules "necessary to carry out" that duty; the Part 121 framework already exists; operationalization is the residual step.

## VI. The Ratchet Map: Where *Loper* Hurts, Where It Helps

### A. Where *Loper* HURTS (the Note's argument)

1. **Deferred reliance on HUD's own interpretation is gone.** If the Note had been prepared to argue that HUD's 1989 Part 121 rulemaking should receive Chevron deference against contrary congressional-intent arguments, that move is unavailable. *Loper* requires de novo review, so Part 121 gets weight only insofar as its text tracks the best reading of the FHA. In practice this is a small cost: Part 121 on its face tracks § 3608(e)(6) faithfully.

2. **Shadow Skidmore risk.** Under the emerging post-*Loper* practice, agency interpretations can still receive "persuasive" weight proportional to their quality, consistency, and longevity. HUD's 37-year failure to operationalize disability fields on Form HUD-27061 will be offered by opponents as evidence of HUD's considered view that § 3608(e)(6) does not require disability-disaggregated collection. But this inverts the usual Skidmore factors: HUD has no *interpretation* of § 3608(e)(6), only a *pattern of non-implementation*. Pattern is not interpretation. Courts post-*Loper* have generally been unwilling to treat sub-regulatory inaction as Skidmore-worthy. See Loper Bright, 144 S. Ct. at 2262 (distinguishing "the careful consideration of agency views" from automatic deference).

3. **Generalized anti-agency climate.** Post-*Loper* circuit courts have vacated a number of agency rules — SEC Private Fund Advisers Rule (Nat'l Ass'n of Priv. Fund Mgrs. v. SEC, 103 F.4th 1097 (5th Cir. 2024)); portions of EEOC's 2024 Harassment Guidance (Texas v. EEOC, No. 2:24-CV-00173-Z, 2025 WL 1481250 (N.D. Tex. May 15, 2025)); CEQ's NEPA regulations (Marin Audubon Soc'y v. FAA, 121 F.4th 902 (D.C. Cir. 2024)). If a court treats the Note's proposed reading as "expanding" HUD's authority, the climate is hostile.

### B. Where *Loper* HELPS (the Note's argument)

1. **Textual § 3608(e)(6) argument is strengthened.** The de novo obligation to read statutory text without deference to agency practice is precisely what the Note needs. Where the text is mandatory ("shall"), de novo review enforces the mandate; where the text enumerates "handicap" alongside "race," de novo review gives "handicap" the same operational weight.

2. **HUD's inaction loses deference cover.** Pre-*Loper*, HUD's omission of disability from Form HUD-27061 could be framed as a reasonable agency choice entitled to deference. Post-*Loper*, inaction is not interpretation; there is nothing for a court to defer to. If the Note frames the claim as *failure to perform a statutory duty* rather than *disagreement with an agency interpretation*, *Loper* cuts strongly for the Note.

3. **APA § 706(1) / mandamus pathways are sharpened.** Agency action "unlawfully withheld" is compelled under 5 U.S.C. § 706(1). Post-*Loper*, the threshold question — whether the statute imposes a duty — is answered de novo. Where § 3608(e)(6) is read for its plain text, the duty is clear, and § 706(1) relief becomes a cleaner fit.

4. **"Best reading" framing preempts the major-questions objection.** Because § 3614a expressly names "collection . . . of appropriate data" as a permitted rule type, the delegation is not "vague" or "ancillary" in the MQD sense. *Loper Bright* at 2263 (citing West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S. 697 (2022)). The Note should frame the asserted authority as narrow, specific, and textually named — not as an expansive new power.

5. **Reframing from "compel HUD to do something new" to "compel HUD to do what the text already requires" is doctrinally cleaner.** *Loper*'s central move — courts decide what statutes mean — is a pro-judicial-enforcement move when the underlying duty is mandatory. The Note should lean into this reframing.

### C. Net vector

On the five-factor balance, *Loper* is a net positive for the Note's authority argument. The single largest reason: HUD has nothing to defend. It has not issued a formal interpretation of § 3608(e)(6) that narrows the disability reporting duty. Its inaction cannot itself be deference-worthy interpretation. And the text is facially mandatory. This is the unusual case where the destruction of Chevron deference is net-pro-regulatory, because the "agency interpretation" that deference used to protect was the agency's choice *not to regulate*.

**Critical finding — explicitly flagged gap:** As of the research cutoff for this memo (April 2026), **no published post-*Loper Bright* federal appellate decision has squarely held that statutory data-reporting language compels an agency to *collect* data it has failed to collect**. Several decisions (*Tennessee v. EEOC*; 8th Cir. SEC climate case) hover near this question, but none directly addresses a statutory mandate phrased as a "shall . . . annually report . . . data on" formulation. This is a gap that works both ways: (1) there is no adverse precedent the Note must distinguish; (2) there is no direct favorable precedent, so the Note's argument is genuinely novel. The Note should present the argument as the straightforward application of ordinary *Loper*-era textualism to a facially unambiguous mandate — not as an exotic expansion of agency-forcing doctrine. This framing posture is the strongest one available.

### Counter-case risk inventory

- **Ohio v. EPA**, 603 U.S. 279 (2024), and related stay-posture rulings emphasize judicial skepticism of agency action claimed under broadly phrased authority. Counter: § 3608(e)(6)'s language is not broadly phrased; it is categorical.
- **West Virginia v. EPA**, 597 U.S. 697 (2022) (MQD). Counter: compelling HUD to collect one field on an existing form is not a "major question." The economic and political significance is modest; it is not a transformative expansion of agency authority.
- **Marin Audubon Soc'y v. FAA**, 121 F.4th 902 (D.C. Cir. 2024). Counter: distinguishable because FHA § 3614a expressly confers data-rulemaking authority on HUD; NEPA conferred no rulemaking authority on CEQ. Cite *Marin Audubon* defensively, not offensively.

The following draft paragraph is ready to drop into the Note's doctrinal section (recommended placement: the section introducing the statutory-authority argument, immediately after the initial framing of § 3608(e)(6)):

> The shift from *Chevron* to *Loper Bright* does not weaken HUD's statutory duty to collect and report disability-disaggregated housing data; it clarifies it. Section 3608(e)(6) directs the Secretary, in mandatory terms, to "annually report to the Congress, and make available to the public, data on the race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, handicap, and family characteristics of persons and households" participating in federally assisted housing. 42 U.S.C. § 3608(e)(6). The text lists handicap alongside race, without differentiation. Section 3614a supplies express rulemaking authority, specifically naming "the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data" as within HUD's delegated zone. *Id.* § 3614a. Before *Loper Bright*, HUD's decades-long omission of disability from Form HUD-27061 might have been sheltered by deference to the agency's implicit reading that the statutory command could be satisfied through aggregate reporting drawn from other sources. After *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), that shelter is gone. Courts must now exercise independent judgment on the meaning of § 3608(e)(6). And the independent meaning is unambiguous: a duty to "annually report . . . data on . . . handicap" imposes, at minimum, a duty to collect the data to be reported. HUD's thirty-seven-year failure to operationalize 24 C.F.R. Part 121's disability fields is not a discretionary agency choice subject to deference. It is a textual violation subject to de novo review. The *Loper Bright* ratchet runs in one direction here: the doctrine's central move — stripping agencies of deference for ambiguous interpretations — is monotonically favorable to this argument because HUD has no formal interpretation to defend, only a pattern of non-implementation. Pattern is not interpretation; inaction is not *Skidmore*-worthy reasoning. The statutory command is facially mandatory; the regulatory framework to implement it has existed since 1989; the only missing step is operationalization. *Loper Bright* makes that step judicially compellable.

## IX. Specific Risks: Counter-Cases and Textualist Objections

### A. Textualist objections the Note must preempt

1. **"May" in § 3614a defeats compulsion.** An opponent may argue that § 3614a's "may make rules" language precludes compulsion of rulemaking. *Response:* The compulsion target is § 3608(e)(6)'s "shall . . . annually report," not § 3614a's "may make rules." § 3614a supplies the *authority* to act; § 3608(e)(6) supplies the *duty* to act. Section 3535(d)'s "shall . . . make such rules . . . as may be necessary to carry out his functions, powers, and duties" supplies a backup rulemaking duty. The structural argument holds.

2. **§ 3608(f) reading-out argument.** Section 3608(f) scopes § 3608(e)(6) to "statutes and Executive orders" listed therein. An opponent may argue that disability data reporting is not within § 3608(f)'s enumerated statutes. *Response:* The FHA itself (§ 3608 et seq.) is listed in § 3608(f). The FHA as amended in 1988 added "handicap" to § 3604's protected classes; § 3608(e)(6)'s "handicap" parallels that addition. Reading § 3608(f) to exclude disability from § 3608(e)(6) would render the word "handicap" in § 3608(e)(6) surplusage — violating the canon against surplusage, which *Loper*-era courts treat as authoritative.

3. **Aggregate-reporting substitution argument.** An opponent may argue that HUD can satisfy § 3608(e)(6) through aggregate reporting from AHS, ACS, or other Census data. *Response:* "Data on . . . handicap . . . of persons and households who are applicants for, participants in, or beneficiaries of programs administered by the Department" cannot be satisfied by data on the general population. The statutory referent is programs HUD administers; only HUD knows who those participants are; therefore only HUD-collected participant-level data satisfies the text.

4. **Major Questions Doctrine objection.** *Response:* Modest, textually specified, long-contemplated (since 1989) rulemaking is not the sort of "elephant in a mousehole" that triggers MQD. The authority is neither "vast" nor "transformative" — it concerns one demographic field on an existing data-collection form.

### B. Counter-cases to expect

- *Marin Audubon Soc'y v. FAA* (distinguishable on express-delegation grounds).
- *Texas v. EEOC* (distinguishable: it concerned sub-regulatory guidance on sex discrimination categories, not mandatory statutory data fields).
- *Nat'l Ass'n of Priv. Fund Mgrs. v. SEC* (distinguishable: SEC's defect was rulemaking that exceeded Dodd-Frank's textual scope; HUD's claimed authority is within § 3608(e)(6)'s express text).

### C. The "who enforces" risk

Even if the Note prevails on statutory authority, the question of *who* compels HUD remains. Candidates: (1) disability-rights plaintiffs via APA § 706(1); (2) Congressional oversight (non-judicial); (3) HUD-internal policy shift. The Note should briefly flag that the statutory authority argument is necessary but not sufficient; the remedial architecture is a separate analytic step. (This is not itself a defect in the Note's authority argument, but honest framing requires naming the gap.)

### D. Residual gap: no direct post-*Loper* precedent compelling data collection

As flagged in Part VII, no published post-*Loper* federal appellate decision has squarely compelled an agency to collect statutorily enumerated data it has failed to collect. The Note should present this not as a weakness but as the natural next step in *Loper*-era textualism — a case of first impression whose resolution is dictated by the text. The Note's contribution is precisely to identify this pathway before it is litigated; this framing enhances the Note's claim to originality for student-research-award purposes.

---

## X. Conclusion

*Loper Bright* is an asymmetric ratchet for the Note's authority argument. It strips deference from HUD's thirty-seven-year pattern of non-operationalization while leaving intact HUD's express, textually specified rulemaking authority under § 3614a. The combination — a mandatory reporting duty in § 3608(e)(6), an express data-rulemaking grant in § 3614a, a belt-and-suspenders rulemaking duty in § 3535(d), and a 1989 regulatory framework in 24 C.F.R. Part 121 already in place — is not merely adequate post-*Loper*. It is stronger than it was under Chevron, because the principal pre-*Loper* defense available to HUD (deference to its practice of not operationalizing Part 121) is exactly the defense that *Loper* abolishes. The Note should embrace *Loper* affirmatively, frame HUD's omission as textual violation rather than interpretive disagreement, and proceed under APA § 706(1) / mandamus principles as the remedial chassis. The argument is novel, but the novelty is the product of *Loper*'s recency, not of any doctrinal overreach. The text has said, since 1988, what the Note now says it says.
