## Bottom line

The new disability-wave coding materially strengthens the current draft's central move away from a broad judicial-hostility story and toward an institutional-failure story. The safest new contribution is not "courts are rejecting disability housing claims on the merits." It is that a large share of the classified disability docket is being sorted through translation, gateway, documentation, and public-process failures that are plausibly responsive to reporting, verification, and institutional-intermediation reforms.

The results fit the current draft especially well where it argues:
1. disability enforcement failure is best understood as pipeline and administrative failure;
2. complaint-driven enforcement is a poor substitute for affirmative administrative visibility;
3. a reinforcement model tied to reporting and targeted verification is more defensible than open-ended replication.

## What the completed disability-wave results most strengthen

### 1. The draft's "not a clean merits-collapse story" framing is now much stronger

Across the 1,256 successful disability-wave classifications:
- `TRANSLATION` = 253 cases (20.1%)
- `PROCEDURAL_GATEWAY` = 230 cases (18.3%)
- combined, those two families account for 483 cases (38.5%)
- `MERITS_EVIDENCE` is only 55 cases (4.4%)
- `NO_FAILURE_PLAINTIFF_WIN` is the single largest family at 353 cases (28.1%)

That distribution strongly supports the current draft's claim that the disability docket should not be summarized as a simple merits-rejection domain. The center of gravity is much closer to translation, gateway, and case-formation problems than to clean substantive defeat.

### 2. The draft's pipeline-failure account now has a more precise mechanism layer

Top coded mechanisms are:
- `CLAIM_SURVIVES_OR_PLAINTIFF_PREVAILS`: 383 (30.5%)
- `JURISDICTION_OR_STANDING`: 146 (11.6%)
- `ELEMENTS_NOT_TIED_TO_FACTS`: 117 (9.3%)
- `REQUEST_NOT_ALLEGED`: 108 (8.6%)

The note can now say more specifically what pipeline failure looks like inside opinions: not just loss, but repeated failures to convert grievances into defendant-linked, request-specific, procedurally viable FHA claims.

This dovetails with the existing pro se memo: the docket is being thinned before doctrine develops, and the thinning mechanism is disproportionately procedural and translational.

### 3. The draft's reinforcement thesis gains direct support from the institutional-function coding

Most common missing institutional functions:
- `THEORY_SELECTION`: 647 (51.5%)
- `FACT_DEVELOPMENT`: 642 (51.1%)
- `DOCUMENT_ASSEMBLY`: 460 (36.6%)
- `JURISDICTIONAL_TRIAGE`: 309 (24.6%)

This is highly usable for the note because it suggests the main problem is often not the total absence of a grievance, but the absence of institutions that can translate a grievance into a record, a theory, and a procedurally viable claim. That directly reinforces the current draft's move from complaint dependence toward internalized screening, reporting, and verification functions.

### 4. The results sharpen, rather than weaken, the argument for administrative observability

Most common administratively observable patterns:
- `NOTICE_OR_TIMELINE_RECORD`: 941 (74.9%)
- `DOCUMENTED_REQUEST`: 832 (66.2%)
- `PUBLIC_PROGRAM_RULE_OR_DECISION`: 425 (33.8%)
- `DENIAL_OR_NO_RESPONSE_RECORD`: 256 (20.4%)
- `HEARING_OR_GRIEVANCE_RECORD`: 242 (19.3%)
- `PHYSICAL_ACCESS_BARRIER`: 126 (10.0%)

This is an important refinement. The safest reading is not that disability disputes are wholly factually invisible. It is that many are administratively legible in principle, yet the system still fails to convert those observable facts into consistent enforcement or durable doctrine. That refinement actually helps Part II and Part III: the need is less to invent facts than to capture, standardize, and verify already-recurring kinds of facts.

### 5. The public-process angle is now a real note-strengthening lane, not just a side observation

The completed run flags `503` cases (40.0%) as `public_process_failure_flag = true`.

That is a large enough slice to support the current draft's use of public and quasi-public process failure as a bridge between empirical pipeline failure and the proposed reinforcement model. Combined with the separate public-defendant memo, the note can credibly argue that a substantial share of disability housing breakdowns arise in process-heavy, authority-fragmented institutional settings where reporting, record-linkage, and verification duties matter most.

### 6. The fixability coding supports the draft's institutional modesty

Tier/fixability coding:
- `BOTH`: 681
- `TIER2_INSTITUTIONAL_INTERMEDIATION`: 284
- combined `BOTH` + `TIER2_INSTITUTIONAL_INTERMEDIATION` = 965 cases (76.8%)
- `TIER1_REPORTING_ARCHITECTURE` alone is only 7 cases

The best inference is not that reporting alone solves the disability docket. It is that reporting/verification plus institutional intermediation looks responsive to much more of the observed problem set than doctrinal revolution alone. That fits the current draft's narrow, floor-not-ceiling positioning.

## Which current-draft claims should be narrowed or framed carefully

### 1. Be careful with "administrative invisibility"

The draft currently uses invisibility language well, but the new coding suggests the strongest version is qualified invisibility, not total invisibility. Many disputes already contain observable ingredients: requests, dates, letters, denials, public rules, hearings, barriers. The failure is that HUD and related institutions do not systematically collect, standardize, verify, or route this information.

Safest framing:
- not "the facts are absent"
- but "the system lacks durable administrative infrastructure for capturing and verifying recurring disability-relevant facts"

### 2. Do not overstate a generalized merits-hostility thesis

The new results continue to support the current draft's narrower position. They do not justify saying that disability claims broadly fail because courts reject disability rights on the merits. Too much of the coded docket is translation, gateway, or mixed process failure for that stronger claim.

Safest framing:
- the docket is procedurally and institutionally filtered;
- that filtering suppresses merits development;
- represented cases and plaintiff-win rows remain too substantial to support a flat "merits collapse" narrative.

### 3. Avoid implying that Tier 1 reporting alone is the whole answer

Because only 7 cases were coded as `TIER1_REPORTING_ARCHITECTURE` alone, the strongest use of the disability-wave results is to justify a combined model: reporting and verification as the administrative floor, with intermediation, triage, and record-building still doing major work.

Safest framing:
- reporting and verification are necessary administrative preconditions;
- they are not a complete substitute for institutional support, intake, and translation.

### 4. Be careful with any claim that the new coding proves doctrinal novelty is the dominant problem

Doctrinal-gap coding is mostly low or none:
- `LOW`: 576
- `NONE`: 277
- `MEDIUM`: 385
- `HIGH`: 18

So the better note claim is not that most cases fail because doctrine is radically underdeveloped in every sense. Rather, the doctrine-production problem is downstream: procedural and institutional filtering reduces the number of cases that receive mature doctrinal treatment, especially for more structural or public-process theories.

### 5. Acknowledge completion limits clearly

The run is strong enough for note use, but it is not literally complete in the sense of perfect coverage:
- 1,330 total requests
- 1,256 successful classifications
- 74 hard errors still flagged for rerun

The note should therefore treat the disability-wave coding as a strong completed tranche rather than a final universe. That said, the error rate is small enough that the headline directional findings appear stable.

## Safest exact empirical propositions the note can now state

These are the strongest note-facing propositions to use substantially as written.

1. Within the completed disability-wave classification tranche, the dominant coded failure families are translation and procedural-gateway failure, not merits-evidence failure.

2. In the same tranche, the most common missing institutional functions are theory selection, fact development, document assembly, and jurisdictional triage, which supports reading the disability docket as an institutional-conversion problem rather than a pure merits-rejection problem.

3. Many disability disputes appear administratively legible in principle: documented requests, notice/timeline records, public-program decisions, and denial/no-response records recur at high rates in the classified cases.

4. The practical bottleneck is therefore often not the total absence of facts, but the absence of systems that collect, verify, and route disability-relevant facts into enforceable administrative and litigation pathways.

5. Public-process failure is not peripheral. Roughly 40% of successful classifications carry the public-process flag, which supports the note's focus on institutional fragmentation and process-heavy public decision environments.

6. The disability-wave results fit best with a combined reform logic: reporting and verification as an administrative floor, plus institutional intermediation and record-building functions. They do not support a claim that disclosure alone will fix the docket.

7. The new coding further supports the current draft's claim that complaint-driven enforcement is a poor substitute for affirmative administration, because the recurring breakdowns are concentrated in translation, documentation, and gateway stages that a purely reactive system handles badly.

## Best places to use the new findings in the current draft

### Part II
Use the disability-wave results to sharpen the measurement-failure / pipeline-failure distinction:
- measurement failure: HUD does not systematically capture the recurring categories of disability-relevant facts;
- pipeline failure: even where those facts exist in some form, cases break down in translation, record-building, and procedural conversion.

### Part II.C-D
Add the new mechanism layer as the best explanation of why complaint-driven enforcement underperforms:
- request not adequately alleged;
- elements not tied to facts;
- jurisdiction/standing barriers;
- missing theory selection and document assembly.

### Part III
Use the observability coding to support the claim that reinforcement is administratively realistic. The system is not starting from zero; it is failing to standardize and verify fact patterns that already recur.

### Part IV / objections
Use the fixability coding to resist overclaiming. The note can candidly say its model is a floor because the results suggest many failures require both reporting architecture and institutional intermediation, not reporting alone.

## Short drafting takeaway

If the note wants one new sentence-level synthesis from this run, it is this:

The completed disability-wave coding suggests that the modern disability FHA docket is not best understood as a field of merits rejection so much as a field of failed translation: requests, notices, denials, public-program decisions, and accessibility facts often exist in recognizable form, but the institutions needed to turn those facts into verified compliance and mature doctrine are missing or degraded.

That is the cleanest bridge from the new results to the current draft's enacted-floor and reinforcement theory.
