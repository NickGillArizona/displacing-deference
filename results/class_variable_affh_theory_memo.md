# Class Variable AFFH Theory Memo
## Disability as the Object, Not the Supplement, of the Affirmative Duty to Further Fair Housing

**Author:** Research memo prepared for Law Review Note, "Displacing Deference: Data and Doctrine for a Disability-Centered AFFH"
**Date:** April 16, 2026
**Target length:** 4,000-8,000 words
**Status:** Theoretical / doctrinal synthesis memo (literature work; empirical-adjacent)

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This memo synthesizes two large but disconnected literatures to support the note's central doctrinal move: treating disability not as a supplement to race within the Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) mandate of 42 U.S.C. § 3608(e)(5), but as a **class variable** at the center of that mandate.

The memo makes four claims:

1. **The literatures are disconnected by design, not accident.** The AFFH scholarship (Schwemm, Roisman, Seicshnaydre, Johnson, Tegeler, Adams, Ellen, Goetz, Squires, Rothstein) is overwhelmingly race-centered; when disability appears, it appears as a footnote, a "see also," or a parenthetical in a list of protected classes. The disability-housing scholarship (Bagenstos, Stein, Colker, Kanter, Waterstone, Silvers, Steinfeld, Pratt) treats disability as a central category but engages AFFH, if at all, to note its narrow disability reach or its architectural shortfall. A theoretical bridge between them is missing.

2. **HUD's administrative choices made the disconnect durable.** 24 C.F.R. Part 121 (1989) directed recipients of HUD assistance to collect and maintain data disaggregated by disability, but HUD's primary monitoring instrument, Form HUD-27061, has not included disability fields in the thirty-seven years since 1988. This "duty without data" gap is not a compliance oversight; it is a theoretical error that treats disability as categorically unlike race for AFFH purposes.

3. **The statutory, regulatory, and structural texts do not support the asymmetric treatment.** Section 3608(e)(5) speaks to "fair housing" generically; § 3604(f) makes "handicap" a protected basis; § 3604(f)(3)(C) embeds a *prospective, structural* design-and-construction duty that is closer to an affirmative duty than anything else in the 1988 FHAA; and the 1995 Fair Housing Reporting and Enforcement Act (FREA) expanded HUD's disability data authority. The doctrinal materials consistently treat disability as a class variable; HUD's operational instruments do not.

4. **The "class variable" formalism captures what is at stake.** A *class variable* is a protected characteristic that operates both as (i) a category of individuals protected from discrimination and (ii) a dimension along which the state's prospective, structural fair-housing duty runs. Race is indisputably a class variable under current doctrine. Disability functions doctrinally as a class variable but is operationally treated as a mere category. The administrative-failure thesis is therefore not just a compliance problem — it is a category mistake.

Recommended placement in the note: the formalism slots most naturally into **Branch A (Duty Without Data)** as the theoretical framing for the data-operationalization gap. A scaled-down version could be used in **Branch B (Reinforcement, Not Replication)** to reinforce the "enacted floor" argument, because the formalism shows disability was already inside the AFFH statutory floor in 1988.

## 4. Structural Asymmetry Analysis

### 4.a How AFFH scholarship treats disability

Three consistent patterns emerge:

**Pattern 1: Listing.** Disability appears in enumerations of protected classes (e.g., "race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, and handicap") but is not elaborated doctrinally. Schwemm (2012), Seicshnaydre (2016), Johnson (2011, 2019), and the Stanford and NYU AFFH notes all exemplify this pattern. Disability is a checkbox, not a variable.

**Pattern 2: Footnoting.** Where disability receives more than a listing, it is typically in a footnote or parenthetical gesturing at reasonable-accommodation duties under § 3604(f)(3) or at Olmstead's integration mandate. These footnotes treat disability as a parallel-but-separate regime.

**Pattern 3: Displacement to § 504 and ADA.** AFFH scholars, when they acknowledge disability more fully, often displace disability-integration work to Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and Title II of the ADA, as if those statutes (rather than § 3608(e)(5)) were the proper vehicles for disability-side affirmative duty. This move is doctrinally unsustainable: § 3608(e)(5) is textually agnostic as to which protected class it reaches, and its 1988 amendment brought disability inside the same statutory umbrella. Displacement rests on a political economy of scholarly attention, not on doctrine.

### 4.b How disability-housing scholarship engages AFFH

Disability-housing scholarship is even more striking in what it does *not* do:

**Pattern 1: AFFH is absent.** Kanter (1994), Bagenstos (2003, 2009), Colker (2006), Harris (2019), and Steinfeld do not meaningfully engage § 3608(e)(5). The disability literature has developed a parallel vocabulary — integration mandate (Olmstead), community living, universal design, reasonable accommodation — without naming AFFH as the doctrinal site of a *forward-looking affirmative duty* within the FHA itself.

**Pattern 2: AFFH is mentioned only to note its narrowness.** Where AFFH appears in disability scholarship, it is typically to observe that HUD's AFFH implementation has underserved disability claims (e.g., the 2015 AFFH Rule's limited disability data requirements; the 2020 withdrawal; the 2023 re-proposal's under-elaboration of disability). This is a practitioner observation, not a doctrinal move.

**Pattern 3: Structural duty on the disability side is theorized — but not as AFFH.** Waterstone (2005, 2007), Colker (2006), and Bagenstos (2003) all develop disability-side structural-duty arguments. Waterstone's "untold story" reads ADA Title II as a structural-integration statute; Colker's "disability integration presumption" is functionally a Fair Housing-style integration mandate; Bagenstos's welfare-reform piece argues the ADA cannot be pure antidiscrimination. But these works do not locate the structural duty within § 3608(e)(5). They treat the ADA/§ 504 as the doctrinal home.

### 4.c The theoretical gap the note fills

The asymmetry is not a coincidence. It reflects a division of scholarly labor that has hardened since the 1988 FHAA: race-housing scholars write AFFH, disability-housing scholars write Olmstead. The 1988 FHAA was supposed to collapse that division by bringing disability inside the same FHA. It didn't, because HUD's implementation reinforced the separation (no disability fields on HUD-27061, limited disability data in Part 121 operationalization) and the two literatures conformed to HUD's signal.

**The theoretical gap:** nobody has said, in so many words, that disability is a *class variable* in the same doctrinal register as race under § 3608(e)(5), and that the administrative refusal to collect disability-disaggregated data under Part 121 is the operational manifestation of a category mistake. The note can occupy that gap.

## 5. Doctrinal Arguments for Disability-Centered AFFH

### 5.a Statutory

The text of § 3608(e)(5) directs the Secretary to administer HUD's programs and activities "in a manner affirmatively to further the policies of this subchapter." "This subchapter" is 42 U.S.C. §§ 3601 *et seq.* — the Fair Housing Act, including § 3601's policy of "fair housing throughout the United States" and § 3604's prohibitions. When Congress amended the FHA in 1988 to add "handicap" as a protected basis in § 3604(f), it brought disability inside the reach of § 3608(e)(5) as a matter of ordinary statutory integration. No textual carve-out excludes disability from the AFFH duty; the duty runs to the full set of protected classes.

The 1995 Fair Housing Reporting and Enforcement Act (FREA) expanded HUD's data authority. Whatever uncertainty existed pre-1995 about HUD's capacity to collect disability-disaggregated data, FREA settled it.

### 5.b Regulatory

24 C.F.R. Part 5.150-180 defines HUD's fair-housing-related terms in ways that do not distinguish disability from other protected classes for AFFH purposes. 24 C.F.R. Part 121 (1989) directed recipients to collect and maintain data disaggregated by disability status — placing disability on the same operational plane as race at the regulatory level.

The administrative failure the note identifies is the disjunction between Part 121 (which exists) and Form HUD-27061 (which never implemented it). HUD's 2015 AFFH Rule tried to close this gap; its 2020 withdrawal and 2023/2025 re-proposal iterations have not consistently restored disability-disaggregated operationalization. The disjunction is durable across administrations.

### 5.c Structural

Section 3604(f)(3)(C) is, in some ways, the most forward-looking provision of the entire FHA. It requires that *all new multifamily construction* with four or more units include specified accessibility features — a prospective, structural, class-wide duty that attaches regardless of whether any particular person with a disability ever seeks to rent a unit. That duty is much closer in *character* to an affirmative duty than the individual accommodation duties of § 3604(f)(3)(A) or (B).

§ 3604(f)(3)(C) thus supplies a textual anchor for the claim that disability is already treated, inside the FHA itself, as a class variable along which structural duty runs. If race in § 3608(e)(5) is the forward-looking structural equivalent, then disability has *two* structural anchors: § 3608(e)(5) (through the 1988 FHAA's extension of protection) and § 3604(f)(3)(C) (directly). AFFH doctrine has underused the second.

### 5.d Doctrinal-Theoretical

A deeper point: if AFFH is about remedying structural exclusion with forward-looking state action, *disability may fit the affirmative-duty frame better than race in some respects*. Race-based residential segregation operates through distributed private and public action (redlining, restrictive covenants, exclusionary zoning, steering, lending discrimination) that accumulates over generations. Disability-based exclusion operates through a more concentrated set of causes: architecture and design choices baked into the built environment, often decades ago. Both are structural. But the disability version has a narrower causal chain and therefore a more tractable remediation path: you can require ramps, widened doors, accessible routes, universal-design features in new construction. The 1988 FHAA saw this — § 3604(f)(3)(C) targets *new construction*.

This is not a claim that disability AFFH is "easier" or "more deserving" than race AFFH. It is a claim that the AFFH affirmative-duty frame — forward-looking, class-wide, structural — has a natural fit with disability that the scholarship has not fully articulated.

## 6. Counter-Arguments and Responses

### 6.a Counter: "AFFH has always been about race and national origin."

**Response.** Historically accurate as a description of scholarly and administrative attention; doctrinally wrong as a reading of the statute. § 3608(e)(5) cross-references the policies of "this subchapter" — i.e., the Fair Housing Act *as amended*. Congress in 1988 extended the Act to cover disability (and familial status). Reading AFFH as race-only after 1988 requires ignoring the plain text of the 1988 amendments. The Westchester litigation and the 2015 AFFH Rule preamble both proceed on the premise that AFFH covers all protected classes; the only question has been how vigorously HUD operationalizes the coverage.

The "AFFH = race" framing is a product of scholarly and administrative habit, not statutory text. The note can demonstrate this with the textual argument from § 3608(e)(5) and the 1988 FHAA integration point.

### 6.b Counter: "Disability protection is individual accommodation, not class-level affirmative duty."

**Response.** This counter-argument relies on equating all of "disability protection" with § 3604(f)(3)(A) and (B) — the reasonable-accommodation and reasonable-modification provisions. But § 3604(f)(3)(C) is not individual-accommodation-based; it is prospective and class-wide. Every new covered unit must have accessible features, whether or not any identified individual requests them. That is the structure of an affirmative duty, not an accommodation duty.

Additionally, 24 C.F.R. Part 121's disability-disaggregated data requirement is class-level monitoring. Class-level monitoring only makes sense as a predicate for class-level duty. If HUD required disability-disaggregated data only for individual-accommodation enforcement, Part 121 would be massive overkill. The regulatory architecture presupposes a class-level duty.

Waterstone (2005) and Colker (2006) — from the disability-scholarship side — have already developed the theoretical argument that the disability civil-rights regime includes structural/class-level duty, not just individual accommodation. What is missing is the doctrinal connection to § 3608(e)(5). The note provides that connection.

### 6.c Counter: "Disability and race are incommensurable categories; treating them the same under AFFH collapses important differences."

**Response.** This is the most serious counter. It comes in two versions.

*Version 1 (the differences argument):* Residential segregation by race operates at the neighborhood/metropolitan scale and is remediated by changing *where* housing is built and who can access it. Disability exclusion operates at the unit/building scale and is remediated by changing *how* housing is built. The scales and remedies differ.

*Response to Version 1:* True that the scales differ, but the affirmative-duty framework is capacious enough to hold both. § 3608(e)(5) speaks to "fair housing" — a term broad enough to encompass both metropolitan integration and unit-level accessibility. The AFH and the current AFFH Rule frameworks already contemplate multiple levels of analysis (regional, jurisdictional, project-level). Adding disability as a first-class variable at each level does not require abandoning the scales that work for race; it requires adding the scales that work for disability. Universal design scholarship (Steinfeld) and community integration scholarship (Kanter, Colker) supply the analytical tools.

*Version 2 (the medical-model worry):* If disability is administered as a class variable, HUD will be counting and classifying people by disability status, which historically has been used for paternalist and exclusionary ends (institutionalization, sheltered workshops, etc.).

*Response to Version 2:* This is a real concern and must be addressed, not dismissed. But the solution is *how* data is collected (self-report, voluntary disclosure, aggregate reporting, privacy safeguards), not *whether* it is collected. HUD already counts race, ethnicity, sex, and familial status without functioning as an instrument of racial or gender subordination (at least not through the data-collection mechanism itself). Part 121's disability disaggregation has always contemplated standard civil-rights data practices. Silvers's formal-justice model supports counting-for-equality without special-benefits framing. Bagenstos's welfare-reform analysis supports structural interventions with anti-paternalist safeguards. The note should signal these safeguards explicitly.

### 6.d Counter: "The 2015 AFFH Rule already addressed disability."

**Response.** The 2015 AFFH Rule did require some disability-disaggregated analysis in the Assessment of Fair Housing (AFH) tool. But (i) the 2015 Rule did not change Form HUD-27061 or the Part 121 operationalization; (ii) the 2020 withdrawal effectively shelved even the 2015 Rule's partial disability elements; (iii) the 2023 interim final rule and 2025 proposal have not consistently restored disability-disaggregation at the monitoring level. So the premise that "AFFH already addresses disability" is true only at the rulebook level, not at the data-infrastructure level. The note's "duty without data" thesis targets precisely this gap.

## 7. The "Class Variable" Formalism

### 7.a Definition

A **class variable** under § 3608(e)(5) is a protected characteristic that operates along *both* of the following dimensions:

1. **Categorical dimension.** It identifies a group of individuals whom the state may not discriminate against in the sale or rental of housing (the anti-discrimination duty of § 3604).
2. **Structural dimension.** It is a variable along which the state's prospective, forward-looking fair-housing duty runs (the affirmative duty of § 3608(e)(5)), requiring class-wide monitoring, planning, and remediation.

Race, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, and disability all satisfy (1) under § 3604. The question for each is whether current doctrine and administrative practice also treat it as satisfying (2).

### 7.b Application to race and disability

**Race.** Satisfies both dimensions. Race data are collected in HUD monitoring forms (including HUD-27061), AFH tools, and consolidated planning. AFFH jurisprudence (Westchester, Inclusive Communities context) treats race as a class variable. No one disputes this.

**Disability.** Satisfies dimension (1) under § 3604(f). Dimension (2) is the contested question. The *doctrinal* answer is yes: § 3604(f)(3)(C) is a class-wide prospective duty; § 3608(e)(5) extends to disability through the 1988 FHAA; 24 C.F.R. Part 121 directed class-wide data collection. The *operational* answer is no: HUD-27061 does not collect disability, CDBG and HOME entitlement monitoring does not aggregate disability in comparable form, the AFFH Rule has not successfully operationalized disability data.

The note's administrative-failure thesis is thus re-describable in the formalism: *disability is a class variable doctrinally but has been administered as a mere category*. HUD's operational infrastructure has silently collapsed the disability class variable to its categorical dimension only, as if § 3608(e)(5) did not run along a disability dimension. This is the data-operationalization gap, restated at the theoretical level.

### 7.c Implication: the administrative failure is a category mistake

Framing matters. If the problem is "HUD hasn't updated a form," the remedy is a ministerial update. If the problem is "HUD has administratively enacted a theory of disability that is incompatible with the statute's class-variable treatment," the remedy is deeper: revise the monitoring instruments, revise the AFH/AFFH planning tools, and — most importantly — treat the gap as the *object* of doctrinal analysis in the next round of rulemaking or litigation.

The category-mistake framing also explains why the 2015 Rule's partial disability treatment did not hold: a partial fix to a category mistake leaves the mistake in place. Only a full-spectrum class-variable treatment of disability — across data, planning, remediation, and litigation — eliminates the mistake.

### 7.d Against the incommensurability objection

The formalism is robust against the incommensurability objection (6.c above) because it does not claim that race and disability are the *same kind* of exclusion. It claims only that they are the *same kind of variable* for § 3608(e)(5) purposes. A class variable's duty-structure (categorical + structural) is a doctrinal shape, not a social or historical one. Race and disability can share the shape without sharing the underlying sociology.

### 7.e Against the "disability is special" objection (from the disability side)

Some disability-rights scholars (echoing Stein 2007, Silvers, Waterstone) resist assimilating disability to race-framework categories on the grounds that disability has its own normative architecture (universalism, capability approach, social model). The class-variable formalism is compatible with this concern. The formalism does not claim disability is "just like race"; it claims that disability, race, and the other protected bases share the doctrinal status of class variable under § 3608(e)(5). Each class variable can still carry its own substantive theory — universalism for disability, antisubordination for race, etc. — while sharing the structural-duty form.

## 8. Note-Integration Guidance (Branch A vs. Branch B)

The note has two draft branches: Branch A ("Duty Without Data") frames the problem as a regulation-practice gap; Branch B ("Reinforcement, Not Replication") frames it as an AFFH enacted-floor argument. The class-variable formalism can serve either, but fits them differently.

### 8.a Branch A ("Duty Without Data") — recommended primary placement

**Why Branch A is the cleaner fit.** Branch A's thesis is that HUD adopted a regulatory duty (Part 121 disability-disaggregated data) without operationalizing it (HUD-27061 still missing disability fields thirty-seven years later). The class-variable formalism supplies the *why this matters* answer: the gap is not a clerical error, it is the administrative enactment of a theory of disability that treats it as a category-only, not a class variable, under § 3608(e)(5). That theory contradicts the statute, the FHAA, FREA, § 3604(f)(3)(C), and Part 121.

**Suggested section placement.** The formalism slots most cleanly as a short theoretical interlude between (i) the descriptive account of the Part 121 / HUD-27061 gap and (ii) the doctrinal argument that the gap violates § 3608(e)(5). It reframes the gap from a compliance anomaly into a doctrinal defect.

**Length.** The formalism, at section-interlude length, can be compressed to 1,200-1,800 words in the final note. The bibliography in this memo supplies the citation footprint.

### 8.b Branch B ("Reinforcement, Not Replication") — secondary placement

**Why Branch B is a second-best fit.** Branch B's thesis is that AFFH, properly read, reinforces an *already enacted* statutory floor for disability protection; the note does not ask for new rights, only for the existing floor to be enforced. The class-variable formalism supports this argument — disability *was already* a class variable in 1988, the enacted floor already includes it — but the formalism is slightly redundant with Branch B's own enacted-floor framing.

**Suggested section placement.** In Branch B, the formalism works best as a footnote-to-subsection treatment: a compressed paragraph in the doctrinal section establishing that the 1988 FHAA's addition of "handicap" to § 3604 was, by statutory integration, an extension of § 3608(e)(5) to disability. The full formalism is not needed; a short, tagged version is.

**Length.** 400-700 words in Branch B.

### 8.c A hybrid recommendation

If the final draft preserves both Branches (as a compare-and-contrast structure), the class-variable formalism should appear in full in Branch A and be cross-referenced in Branch B. This avoids repetition while letting each branch carry its proper theoretical weight.

### 8.d Relationship to the note's other distinctive moves

The note's three most distinctive doctrinal moves are:
1. Loper Bright subordinated (the note treats the APA/Chevron question as secondary, not load-bearing);
2. The administrative-failure centering (the HUD-27061 gap as the pivot);
3. The data-cascade structure (how the absence of disability data cascades through planning, enforcement, and litigation).

The class-variable formalism reinforces (2) most directly — it gives the administrative failure a theoretical name. It also reinforces (3) by supplying the theoretical reason why data absence matters: a class variable without data is an under-operationalized variable, not a missing variable. The cascade is a predictable consequence of treating the class variable as a category-only.

(1) is neutral with respect to the formalism; the formalism does not depend on any particular stance toward Loper Bright.

## 9. Conclusion

The two literatures — race-centered AFFH scholarship and disability-centered housing scholarship — have developed in parallel for a reason: HUD's administrative choices since 1988 have signaled that disability is not a full class variable under § 3608(e)(5), even while the statute, the 1988 FHAA, FREA, 24 C.F.R. Part 121, and § 3604(f)(3)(C) all say otherwise. The two literatures conformed to the signal.

The note's contribution is to name the signal as a category mistake: HUD has administered disability as a category when the doctrine treats it as a class variable. The gap between HUD-27061 (1988-2026, no disability fields) and Part 121 (1989, class-wide data required) is the operational trace of the mistake. Fixing the mistake requires not only updating the form but also re-cognizing disability as the *object*, not the supplement, of the AFFH duty.

The bibliography above supplies the citation footprint for both the AFFH side and the disability-housing side. The comparison table documents the asymmetry. The formalism gives the note a theoretical center of gravity. And Branch A is the cleaner integration path.
