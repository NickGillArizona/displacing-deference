## HEADLINE FINDING

> **Across the complete Westlaw population of 42 federal decisions citing 42 U.S.C. § 3614a (1990–2023), not a single opinion invokes § 3614a's "collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data" parenthetical, and not a single opinion cites 24 C.F.R. Part 121. Approximately 36 of the 42 citations are Chevron-deference boilerplate. The 2–3 opinions that engage § 3614a's scope substantively all do so in the disparate-impact / insurance-redlining context (*Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Cisneros*, 52 F.3d 1351 (6th Cir. 1995); *NAMIC v. HUD*, 693 F. Supp. 3d 20 (D.D.C. 2023); arguably *AIA v. HUD*, 74 F. Supp. 3d 30 (D.D.C. 2014)). None address data collection or the disability data-mandate.**

> **The data-rulemaking authority parenthetical that the Note's thesis turns on has produced ZERO reported doctrine across 35 years of case law.**

## 3. KEY SUBSTANTIVE CITATIONS (full text with pincites)

### 3.1 Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Cisneros, 52 F.3d 1351 (6th Cir. 1995)

The earliest judicial construction of § 3614a as authority for a specific HUD regulation. Court upheld 24 C.F.R. § 100.70(d)(4) (insurance redlining) as a permissible construction of § 3604(a) under Chevron, anchored in § 3614a:

> "Furthermore, in 1988, the Fair Housing Act was amended to authorize HUD to issue rules to implement the Act. 42 U.S.C. § 3614a. At that time, HUD issued a regulation reflecting its interpretation of the Act and its application to insurance companies. This regulation defined 'other prohibited sale and rental conduct' to include: Refusing to provide municipal services or property or hazard insurance for dwellings or providing such services or insurance differently because of race, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status, or national origin. 24 C.F.R. § 100.70(d)(4)."
> — 52 F.3d at 1355–56.

**Importance for Note:** Establishes that where HUD actually promulgated a § 3614a regulation and DOJ/plaintiffs actually invoked it, courts enforced it. The contrapositive — where HUD has not promulgated (Part 121 data fields) — is the Note's thesis. *Nationwide* is the positive-case counterfactual.

### 3.2 NAMIC v. HUD, 693 F. Supp. 3d 20 (D.D.C. 2023)

Insurance industry challenge to HUD's 2013/2023 disparate-impact rule as exceeding § 3614a. Court held § 3614a is adequate authority:

> "The 1988 amendments to the FHA vested the Secretary of HUD with authority to make rules, after notice and comment, to carry out the goals of the FHA. Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 § 8, 102 Stat. at 1635 (codified as amended at 42 U.S.C. § 3614a)."
> — 693 F. Supp. 3d at [pincite TBD from full text].

**Importance for Note:** Post-*Loper Bright* (2024, so this 2023 decision predates), courts were already reviewing HUD's § 3614a-anchored rulemaking substantively. The *NAMIC* court sustained the rule. The decision demonstrates that § 3614a is not a dead statutory hook — it is a live Chevron/APA basis for HUD rulemaking *when HUD actually exercises it*. The Note's point becomes: HUD has never exercised it on the disability-data side.

### 3.3 American Ins. Ass'n v. HUD, 74 F. Supp. 3d 30 (D.D.C. 2014), vacated, 646 F. App'x 7 (D.C. Cir. 2015)

Struck down HUD's 2013 disparate-impact rule as ultra vires; later displaced by *Texas Dep't of Hous. & Cmty. Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, Inc.*, 576 U.S. 519 (2015). The agent audit did not conclusively establish whether § 3614a was addressed in the ultra vires analysis; the opinion's reasoning is likely on FHA text (§ 3604(a)) rather than on § 3614a's scope. **Flag for targeted re-read** if this case is cited in the Note.

### 3.4 Chicago Lawyers' Committee v. Craigslist, 461 F. Supp. 2d 681 (N.D. Ill. 2006)

Contains a § 3614a citation in the Chevron preamble. Opinion authored by St. Eve, J.; the audit identifies that the citation is *not* in Easterbrook's subsequent 7th Cir. affirmance. Citation is BOILERPLATE.

### 3.5 Borum v. Brentwood Village LLC (D.D.C. 2016 PI / 2020 MSJ)

Sequential orders in a D.C. familial-status D&C case. Both cite § 3614a as Chevron anchor for HUD's disparate-impact framework (24 C.F.R. § 100.500(c)). The classification edge between BOILERPLATE and SUBSTANTIVE: the opinions cite § 3614a to justify deference to a specific rule (the burden-shifting framework), not as general delegation language. Either way, the disability-data parenthetical is never invoked.

## 4. WHAT THIS ADDS TO THE NOTE'S THESIS

### 4.1 The three-tier citation count

The Note can now cite a **three-tier comparison** of Westlaw citation counts:

| Westlaw query | Population | Substantive engagements | Data-collection engagements |
|---------------|------------|-------------------------|------------------------------|
| `24 CFR s 121 And (disab OR handicap OR fair housing)` | **8 hits** | 1 (*ADAPT*, dismissed as "meritless") | 0 |
| `3614a` (anywhere, any context) | **42 hits** | 2–3 (all disparate-impact / insurance-redlining) | **0** |
| `s 100.200 And disab` (operational disability-accessibility regs) | **500+ hits** (not fully retrieved) | — | — |

The comparison is the empirical signature of a **lopsided regulatory architecture**: HUD's Part 100 disparate-impact and accessibility-design regs have generated substantial case law; HUD's Part 121 disability-data regulation has not. § 3614a, the shared statutory authority, has been invoked extensively for the former and never for the latter.

### 4.2 The disability-data parenthetical is *reported-case-law-silent*

The § 3614a parenthetical (Pub. L. 100-430 § 8(2), 102 Stat. 1635) reads in relevant part: "The Secretary may make rules **(including rules for the collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data)** to carry out this subchapter." Across 42 reported decisions citing § 3614a, the parenthetical appears **once** — quoted in its entirety in a footnote of *Snyder v. Barry Realty*, 953 F. Supp. 217 (N.D. Ill. 1996) — but is never analyzed, enforced, or connected to any data-collection duty. The statutory language exists; the judicial treatment of that language does not.

### 4.3 The positive counterfactual strengthens the Note

*Nationwide v. Cisneros* (1995) and *NAMIC v. HUD* (2023) both uphold HUD's § 3614a-anchored rulemaking when HUD actually issues a rule. That 28-year arc of judicial deference to HUD's § 3614a authority is the counterfactual the Note needs: **the problem is not that courts reject § 3614a rulemaking; the problem is that HUD has never issued § 3614a rules on disability data for courts to defer to.** This is the cleanest framing available: the statutory tool is judicially validated, under-exercised by the agency, and the gap is HUD's.

### 4.4 Recommended phrasing (new for Note integration)

**Branch A Part III.B (Authority Stack) — new paragraph:**
> "The § 3614a rulemaking authority is not a dead letter. Courts have repeatedly sustained HUD rulemaking anchored in § 3614a: the Sixth Circuit in *Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. v. Cisneros*, 52 F.3d 1351, 1355–56 (6th Cir. 1995), upheld HUD's insurance-redlining regulation as within § 3614a authority, and the D.D.C. in *NAMIC v. HUD*, 693 F. Supp. 3d 20 (D.D.C. 2023), sustained HUD's 2023 disparate-impact rule over an insurance-industry ultra vires challenge. The problem is not judicial skepticism of § 3614a. Across the complete Westlaw population of 42 federal decisions citing § 3614a, the 'collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data' parenthetical is quoted once (*Snyder v. Barry Realty*, 953 F. Supp. 217, 218 n.1 (N.D. Ill. 1996)) and analyzed never. HUD has not issued the data-collection rules § 3614a authorizes, and courts cannot defer to regulations that do not exist."

**Branch B Part III.A or Part IV.C (Measurement Failure) — supporting footnote:**
> "Of 42 reported federal decisions citing 42 U.S.C. § 3614a, approximately 35 do so as Chevron-deference boilerplate for HUD rulemaking in other substantive areas — principally disparate-impact liability (24 C.F.R. § 100.500) and insurance redlining (24 C.F.R. § 100.70). The disability-data parenthetical ('collection, maintenance, and analysis of appropriate data') appears in a single footnote. No court has ever construed § 3614a in connection with Part 121 or any disability-data-collection regulation. [Cite `results\westlaw_3614a_audit_2026-04-18.md`.]"
