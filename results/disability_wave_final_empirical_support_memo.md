## Bottom line

The final resolved disability-wave tranche materially strengthens the draft's institutional-failure account, but it supports a narrower and more precise empirical story than a broad judicial-hostility or merits-collapse thesis.

The safest note-facing reading of the resolved 1,330-case tranche is this:

1. the coded disability docket is dominated by translation, gateway, documentation, and theory-selection problems rather than clean merits-evidence failure;
2. many disputes are administratively legible in principle because requests, timelines, denials, hearings, policy texts, and public-program decisions recur at high rates;
3. the practical bottleneck is therefore often not absence of underlying facts, but failure to capture, standardize, verify, and route recurring disability-relevant facts into viable enforcement channels;
4. these results fit the draft best when used to reinforce the existing pipeline-failure and administrative-observability story, not to replace it.

Just as important, the fully resolved file removes the earlier "completed tranche but not fully final" qualification. The final disability-wave run contains 1,330 matched records, with zero orphan results, zero malformed parsed responses, and zero hard errors in the merge report.

## What the final resolved 1,330-case results most safely show

### 1. This is not best described as a clean merits-failure docket

In the final resolved 1,330-case tranche:
- `NO_FAILURE_PLAINTIFF_WIN`: 374 cases (28.1%)
- `TRANSLATION`: 268 cases (20.2%)
- `PROCEDURAL_GATEWAY`: 240 cases (18.0%)
- `MERITS_EVIDENCE`: 58 cases (4.4%)
- `FACTUAL_DETAIL`: 24 cases (1.8%)

Combined:
- `TRANSLATION` + `PROCEDURAL_GATEWAY` = 508 cases (38.2%)
- `MERITS_EVIDENCE` + `FACTUAL_DETAIL` = 82 cases (6.2%)

That is the single safest descriptive proposition from the disability-wave coding. The center of gravity is failed conversion of grievances into legally viable cases, not merits rejection after mature claim development.

### 2. The mechanism layer strongly supports a pipeline-failure account

Most common coded mechanisms:
- `CLAIM_SURVIVES_OR_PLAINTIFF_PREVAILS`: 407 (30.6%)
- `JURISDICTION_OR_STANDING`: 149 (11.2%)
- `ELEMENTS_NOT_TIED_TO_FACTS`: 120 (9.0%)
- `REQUEST_NOT_ALLEGED`: 113 (8.5%)
- `UNCLEAR`: 108 (8.1%)
- `MIXED`: 90 (6.8%)

The best note-facing use of these numbers is not to say simply that disability plaintiffs lose. It is to say that opinions repeatedly show breakdowns in pleading translation, procedural fit, and fact-to-element linkage.

### 3. The institutional-function coding is unusually supportive of the draft's thesis

Most common missing institutional functions:
- `THEORY_SELECTION`: 683 (51.4%)
- `FACT_DEVELOPMENT`: 681 (51.2%)
- `DOCUMENT_ASSEMBLY`: 480 (36.1%)
- `JURISDICTIONAL_TRIAGE`: 322 (24.2%)

These are strikingly note-useful findings because they map directly onto the draft's argument that the disability docket is being filtered by degraded intake, translation, screening, and record-building functions. They support the proposition that complaint-driven enforcement underperforms not only because rights are hard to vindicate, but because institutions are failing to convert recognizable grievances into legally legible records and theories.

### 4. The observability coding supports qualified invisibility, not total invisibility

Most common administratively observable fact patterns:
- `NOTICE_OR_TIMELINE_RECORD`: 979 (73.6%)
- `DOCUMENTED_REQUEST`: 875 (65.8%)
- `PUBLIC_PROGRAM_RULE_OR_DECISION`: 452 (34.0%)
- `LEASE_OR_POLICY_TEXT`: 296 (22.3%)
- `MEDICAL_OR_PROVIDER_LETTER`: 277 (20.8%)
- `DENIAL_OR_NO_RESPONSE_RECORD`: 272 (20.5%)
- `HEARING_OR_GRIEVANCE_RECORD`: 250 (18.8%)
- `PHYSICAL_ACCESS_BARRIER`: 137 (10.3%)
- `INSPECTION_OR_MEASUREMENT_DATA`: 54 (4.1%)
- cases with `NONE_APPARENT`: only 83 (6.2%)

This is an important refinement to the draft's rhetoric. The strongest formulation is not that disability disputes lack facts. It is that the administrative system lacks durable infrastructure for collecting, standardizing, and verifying recurring fact patterns that already appear in recognizable form.

### 5. The public-process dimension is substantial enough to feature prominently

`public_process_failure_flag = true` appears in 531 cases (39.9%).

That is strong support for presenting a significant slice of the disability housing docket as arising in process-heavy, authority-fragmented, often public or quasi-public settings where record-linkage, reporting, hearings, timelines, and institutional routing matter.

### 6. The fixability coding supports a floor-not-ceiling reform logic

Tier/fixability coding:
- `BOTH`: 714 (53.7%)
- `TIER2_INSTITUTIONAL_INTERMEDIATION`: 300 (22.6%)
- `NEITHER`: 278 (20.9%)
- `UNCLEAR`: 31 (2.3%)
- `TIER1_REPORTING_ARCHITECTURE`: 7 (0.5%)

Combined:
- `BOTH` + `TIER2_INSTITUTIONAL_INTERMEDIATION` = 1,014 cases (76.2%)

This sharply supports the current draft's modesty. The results do not say reporting alone is the answer. They say reporting and verification are best defended as an administrative floor, while intermediation, intake, triage, and record-building remain indispensable.

### 7. The doctrinal-gap signal should be used carefully

Doctrinal-gap coding:
- `LOW`: 611 (45.9%)
- `MEDIUM`: 410 (30.8%)
- `NONE`: 289 (21.7%)
- `HIGH`: 20 (1.5%)

The note should not say the disability-wave coding proves a generalized doctrinal vacuum. The better claim is downstream and institutional: procedural and translation failures reduce the number of cases that become clean vehicles for doctrine development, especially in more structural or public-process settings.

## What the current draft should now say

### Safest high-level synthesis

The strongest replacement or reinforcement sentence is:

The final disability-wave coding suggests that the modern disability FHA docket is not best understood as a field of merits rejection so much as a field of failed conversion: requests, notices, denials, policy texts, hearings, and public-program decisions often exist in recognizable form, but the institutions needed to turn those facts into verified compliance, administratively usable records, and mature legal claims are missing or degraded.

### Best note-facing propositions to state affirmatively

1. In the final resolved 1,330-case disability-wave tranche, translation and procedural-gateway failures together account for 38.2% of cases, while merits-evidence and factual-detail failures together account for only 6.2%.
2. The most common coded missing institutional functions are theory selection, fact development, document assembly, and jurisdictional triage, which supports reading the disability docket as an institutional-conversion problem rather than a pure merits-rejection problem.
3. Many disability disputes are administratively legible in principle: documented requests, notice/timeline records, public-program decisions, denial records, policy texts, and hearing records recur frequently in the coded cases.
4. The practical bottleneck is therefore often not that disability-relevant facts do not exist, but that agencies and intermediaries do not reliably capture, standardize, verify, and route those facts.
5. Roughly 39.9% of the final coded tranche carries the public-process-failure flag, supporting the draft's emphasis on fragmented institutional settings rather than only bilateral landlord-tenant disputes.
6. The disability-wave results fit best with a combined reform logic: reporting and verification as an administrative floor, plus institutional intermediation and record-building functions. They do not support a claim that disclosure alone would fix the disability docket.

## What to soften or qualify in the current draft

### 1. Soften absolute invisibility language

Prefer:
- "qualified administrative invisibility"
- "failure of administrative capture and verification"
- "recurring disability-relevant facts are not systematically collected or verified"

Avoid:
- "the facts are absent"
- "the system cannot see these disputes at all"

Why: only 6.2% of coded cases had `NONE_APPARENT` as an observable-fact pattern.

### 2. Do not overstate a generalized merits-hostility thesis

Prefer:
- the docket is heavily filtered through translation and gateway failure;
- merits development is suppressed by institutional and procedural thinning;
- the coded disability docket is not dominated by clean merits-evidence defeat.

Avoid:
- courts are broadly rejecting disability housing claims on the merits;
- the disability docket is principally a merits-collapse story.

Why: `MERITS_EVIDENCE` is only 4.4%, and `MERITS_EVIDENCE` plus `FACTUAL_DETAIL` is only 6.2%.

### 3. Do not imply that reporting architecture alone solves the problem

Prefer:
- reporting and verification are necessary administrative preconditions;
- a reporting floor works best when paired with intermediation and record-building functions.

Avoid:
- disclosure by itself will cure the disability docket.

Why: only 7 cases (0.5%) are coded `TIER1_REPORTING_ARCHITECTURE` alone, while 76.2% are coded `BOTH` or `TIER2_INSTITUTIONAL_INTERMEDIATION`.

### 4. Preserve the composition story, but frame it as dominant rather than exclusive

The composition-tension memo remains important. The current draft should keep composition as the lead aggregate explanation, but the safest phrasing is:
- the dominant aggregate pattern is compositional;
- some within-group deterioration also appears, especially at the pleading gate within stronger represented subsets;
- the disability-wave coding helps explain both by identifying a common mechanism of degraded translation, triage, and record-construction.

### 5. Avoid overselling doctrinal-gap findings

Prefer:
- doctrine-production is being thinned by upstream institutional filtering;
- doctrinal development is constrained because many cases do not arrive as clean vehicles.

Avoid:
- most disability cases fail because doctrine is radically underdeveloped.

Why: `HIGH` doctrinal-gap coding is only 1.5%.

## Recommended draft-use by section

### Part II / measurement failure

Use the observability coding to sharpen the distinction between:
- measurement failure: HUD does not systematically collect recurring disability-relevant facts; and
- pipeline failure: even when those facts exist in letters, requests, timelines, denials, policy texts, hearings, or public-program decisions, they are not reliably converted into enforceable records or viable claims.

### Part II.C-D / complaint dependence and litigation pipeline

Use the mechanism and institutional-function coding to explain what complaint-driven failure looks like inside opinions:
- requests not adequately alleged;
- elements not tied to facts;
- jurisdiction or standing barriers;
- missing theory selection;
- missing document assembly;
- weak fact development and triage.

### Part III / reinforcement as AFFH

Use the observability findings to support administrability. The system is not starting from zero. The recurring problem is failure to standardize and verify fact patterns that already recur across cases.

### Part IV / objections and limits

Use the fixability coding to support candor and narrowness. The empirical results fit a floor-not-ceiling argument: reporting and verification matter, but they do not displace intermediation, counsel, intake, or other institutional supports.

## Final recommendation

The final disability-wave results are strong enough to support a firmer empirical package in the current draft, but only if the draft stays disciplined about what the coding actually shows.

The safest and strongest empirical position is:

The disability-wave data confirm that the present disability FHA docket is best understood as an institutional pipeline problem. The coded cases are dominated by translation, gateway, theory-selection, fact-development, and document-assembly failures; many disputes remain administratively legible in principle; and the results fit best with a model that treats reporting and verification as an administrative floor rather than as a complete substitute for intermediation, counsel, or broader structural reform.

That framing strengthens the note's central claim while reducing vulnerability to overstatement attacks.
