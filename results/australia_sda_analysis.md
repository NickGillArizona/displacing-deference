## Bottom line

Australia's NDIS Specialist Disability Accommodation (SDA) system is a strong international comparator for the note because it already does, in public, much of the data work the proposed U.S. disability-centered AFFH regime is trying to create. The key comparator is not substantive sameness: Australia's SDA regime is a specialist housing-funding and market-monitoring system, while the U.S. proposal is an AFFH verification and anti-concentration regime. The comparator value is administrative. Australia's observability depends in part on a centralized NDIA/NDIS enrollment-and-payment system for a specialist cohort, so it is best used as an administrability comparator rather than as a direct institutional transplant to HUD. Australia publicly tracks enrolled dwellings, design categories, resident caps, regional distribution, providers, unfinished pipeline, and participant demand in recurring national tables.

Using current public NDIA data as at 31 December 2025, Australia reports:

- 15,925 participants with SDA in use,
- 9,577 participants eligible for SDA but not yet using it at that date, a status category rather than a pure shortage/failure count,
- 25,502 total participants with identified SDA need,
- $626.5 million in annualised SDA supports in current plans,
- 683 active SDA providers,
- 13,297 enrolled SDA dwellings,
- 8,462 unfinished (unenrolled) new-build SDA dwellings in the pipeline.

That is the kind of public accessibility-market infrastructure HUD does not currently publish. The note's proposed U.S. regime would not replicate Australia's program design, but Australia shows that a recurring public disability-housing inventory is administratively feasible at national scale. The Australian data also show the limit of data visibility alone: despite this dashboard, 37.6% of the identified SDA-need cohort was still in the administrative "eligible but not yet using SDA" category at year-end 2025, which is not itself a pure shortage/failure metric.

## Main comparison findings

### 1. Australia shows that the core data architecture is feasible

The most important comparator point is simple: Australia already publishes the kind of recurring accessibility-market information HUD does not. The note argues that HUD currently cannot see the accessible-housing problem with anything like adequate precision. Australia shows that a central agency can publish recurring public tables covering stock, demand, geography, design categories, providers, and pipeline.

### 2. Data visibility does not eliminate unmet need

Even with a relatively mature public data system, 9,577 people were still in the administrative "eligible for SDA but not yet using it" category at 31 December 2025, which is not a pure shortage/failure metric. So the policy lesson is not "build a dashboard and the problem is solved." The lesson is narrower and more useful for the note: once the data layer exists, need and mismatch become more legible, regionally traceable, and governable.

### 3. The Australian stock profile is a proxy, not proof, of a less congregate model

Australia's enrolled stock is mostly new build or refurbished new build (70.9%) and mostly 1-2 resident dwellings (67.8%); legacy dwellings are 1.2% by build type, and 6+ resident dwellings are separately 1.2% by resident cap. Those build-type and resident-cap metrics are useful proxies for smaller-scale supply, not proof of non-congregate placement or community-integrated lived outcomes. Still, the profile suggests the feasibility of disability housing supply that is less overtly congregate.

### 4. Australia also shows what the U.S. proposal is designed to do more explicitly

Australia's public dashboard is strong on visibility but weaker, at least in the public data layer itself, on explicit anti-concentration decision rules. The note's U.S. proposal is designed to be stronger here because it couples inventory-building with a community-integration screen and an Availability Audit keyed to higher-opportunity alternatives.

### 5. Category-specific demand data matter, but classification quality remains hard

The Australian system publishes need by design category, which is a major advantage over current HUD practice. But 21.1% of the identified-need cohort still sits in a `Missing` design-category field, and the pipeline is even more concentrated in High Physical Support than the enrolled stock. The U.S. lesson is that counting units alone is not enough; the registry has to be category-specific and quality-controlled.

### 6. The best use of Australia in the note

Australia is best used as a proof-of-administrability comparator, not as a one-to-one legal analogue or direct institutional transplant. Its observability depends in part on a centralized NDIA/NDIS enrollment-and-payment system for a specialist cohort. It is evidence that a national disability-housing authority can publicly maintain:

- a recurring enrolled-unit inventory,
- a demand-side cohort,
- regional supply and need tables,
- design-category-specific reporting,
- and pipeline visibility.

That directly supports the note's claim that HUD's present informational void is not inevitable.
