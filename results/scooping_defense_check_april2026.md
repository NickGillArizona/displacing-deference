## What counts as overlap for this memo

Using the local project materials as the comparison point, the note’s distinctive package is not just “fair housing” or even just “AFFH.” The project is organized around:
- disability-centered AFFH,
- the claim that public systems generate fragmented process facts but fail to convert them into reliable, reviewable verification infrastructure,
- reinforcement-not-replication / enacted-floor framing,
- and the note’s empirical foundation in the disability FHA dataset and the post-2025 enforcement-collapse story.

That means a publication only creates serious scooping risk if it overlaps substantially with several of those elements at once, not merely if it discusses fair housing, segregation, disability, or administrative law in isolation.

## 3. Noah Kazis

### What I found

Kazis is the closest overlap.

Recent items located:
- *The Radical Fair Housing Act*, 111 Va. L. Rev. 491 (2025)
- *Rezoning The Rust Belt* (forthcoming 2026, Washington University Law Review)
- Google Scholar also lists *Local Government Through the Looking Glass: Military Bases as Municipalities* (2026)

The relevant 2025 article is *The Radical Fair Housing Act*. The SSRN/VRL descriptions say the article argues that the FHA has a structural, market-remaking anti-discrimination logic, not merely a transaction-policing logic. The Virginia Law Review page description also expressly references AFFH and Kazis’s earlier work on scrutinizing land-use regulation under the FHA’s AFFH provisions.

That matters. It is real overlap at the level of structural FHA theory, and it is the one piece from the searched set that would need clear acknowledgement in the note.

But the differences are still substantial:
- Kazis is not doing a disability-centered project.
- He is not building the note’s verification-mismatch theory.
- He is not using the disability FHA litigation dataset.
- He is not centered on the post-2025 enforcement withdrawal / pipeline-collapse story.
- His core move is a structural reinterpretation of the FHA as such; the note’s move is to use disability litigation and public-process failure to argue for a disability-centered AFFH implementation frame.

The 2026 piece *Rezoning The Rust Belt* appears to be a land-use/zoning article about postindustrial markets, not AFFH, disability, or verification infrastructure.

### Overlap assessment

Risk level: moderate, but only at a high level of structural fair-housing theory.

Why: *The Radical Fair Housing Act* occupies some of the same doctrinal neighborhood. It is not a scoop of the note’s actual package, but it is important adjacent literature and probably the single most necessary cite-and-distinguish authority in this set.

### Practical implication

The note should explicitly distinguish itself from Kazis along these lines:
- Kazis: general structural theory of the FHA / housing markets / AFFH-adjacent architecture.
- This note: disability-centered AFFH, verification mismatch, administrative failure, and empirically grounded enforcement-pipeline analysis.

## Recommended scooping-defense position for the note

A fair and defensible originality statement after this check is:

- The note is not claiming to be the first structural or AFFH-adjacent FHA article.
- Kazis and Johnson, in particular, already occupy important structural fair-housing and housing-remedies territory.
- What appears original as of April 2026 is the combination of:
  - disability-centered AFFH framing,
  - the verification-mismatch concept,
  - the public-process / administrative-failure account,
  - and the empirical grounding in the disability FHA litigation dataset and post-2025 enforcement collapse.

In other words: there is adjacent literature, and one especially important 2025 fair-housing article by Kazis, but I found no 2026 publication by these authors that displaces the note’s specific thesis.
