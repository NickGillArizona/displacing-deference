## Coding conventions

- Inventory = explicit accessible-unit inventory, count verification, registry, or unit-by-unit accessibility audit/order.
- Occupancy tracking = who occupies accessible units, or in Olmstead matters, voucher utilization / placement / waiting-list tracking.
- Distribution = express § 8.26 dispersal term or broader integration/distribution architecture already coded in the local memo record.
- Public registry = public-facing searchable registry, not an internal list.
- Unclear values are conservative. I did not infer missing decree terms from general practice alone.

## Top-line findings

- Of 22 consent decrees/VCAs/settlements with physical accessibility or accessible-unit components, 59.1% (13) expressly required accessible-unit inventories, count verification, or registries.
- Strict express § 8.26 subset: 66.7% (2 of 3) expressly required accessible-unit inventories, count verification, or registries, and the same 66.7% (2 of 3) also required occupancy / tenant-placement / tenant-matching tracking.
- Broader integration/distribution subset treating Los Angeles's parallel Section 504 orders as separate instruments: 80.0% (4 of 5) required occupancy or waiting-list tracking; collapsing Los Angeles to one legal architecture yields 75.0% (3 of 4).
- Lower-bound finding: The coded record expressly shows courts and agencies ordering accessible-unit inventories, count verification, registries, or unit-by-unit audits at least 13 times. If Olmstead placement-tracking systems and other non-inventory tracking architectures are included, the broader data-infrastructure count rises to 20.
- Section IV implementation overlay cuts in the same direction: Los Angeles reached only about one-quarter of required output at roughly three-quarters of the decree term; PRPHA remained out of compliance after 23 years; Bridgeport generated no public monitoring record; Cambridge and ADAPT succeeded only at much smaller scales.

## Monitoring-duration highlights

- Longest-running accessibility/distribution oversight in the coded set: PRPHA (2003-2026 across successive agreements), HABC (2004 decree modified 2015 with decade-plus monitoring), and Los Angeles (2016 settlement plus 2019 VCA, still active).
- Typical DOJ D&C decrees cluster around 4-5 years where the memo gives a number (Edward Rose 5 years; Dawn 4 years; Quality Built 5-year injunction; JPI 5 years).
- Small-scale success cases were short and bounded: Cambridge completed by 2021; ADAPT completed by May 2006.

## Interpretation

The pattern is consistent across remedy types. DOJ D&C decrees use inventories to make retrofit obligations administrable. Section 504 public-housing decrees layer on tenant placement, waiting-list, vacancy, and accommodation-request tracking whenever recipients must distribute accessible units or keep them occupied by people who need the features. Olmstead settlements do the same thing in functional form by counting supported-housing slots and tracking placements. In other words: outputs are integration inputs. When courts or agencies enforce integration/distribution duties in the coded record, the remedy often formalizes inventory-and-tracking architecture.

## Conservative-coding caveats

- The 13-case lower bound is deliberately conservative. Philadelphia PHA, PRPHA, Cambridge, and Atlanta were recoded from inventory = yes to inventory = unclear because the local memos describe unit targets, waiting-list/reporting duties, or generic tracking language without quoting a separate inventory/count-verification term.
- Quality Built, Design Professionals, Wallace, Chicopee, and ADAPT may also have had additional inventory terms, but the local memos excerpted here do not spell them out, so they were not counted upward.
- The broad 80.0% integration/distribution statistic treats the 2016 and 2019 Los Angeles instruments as separate matters; collapsing them to one legal architecture yields 75.0% (3 of 4).
- NYCHA was excluded from the statistics because the local record identifies only multiple informal monitoring arrangements, not one discrete public decree/VCA with enough coded detail.
- Thompson v. HUD was reviewed for implementation context but excluded from the coded accessibility denominator because it is a desegregation/mobility case rather than an accessible-unit remedy.
