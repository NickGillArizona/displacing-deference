# Public-Process Follow-On Memo v2

Using the completed disability-wave outputs, this memo updates and sharpens the earlier public-defendant process-failure memo. It focuses on the public/quasi-public disability docket, especially the public-process plus doctrinal-gap queue, and identifies the strongest mechanisms, the most important doctrinal gaps, and the cases that now deserve raw-text review or targeted rerun attention.

## Bottom line

The completed disability-wave outputs strengthen the earlier thesis. The public/quasi-public disability docket still looks less like a domain of clear merits rejection and more like a domain of administrative failure filtered through pleading, record, and authority problems. But the queue now lets us say more precisely where the pressure points are:

1. The dominant failure mode is still translation, not total absence of an underlying grievance.
2. The most recurrent missing functions are theory selection, fact development, document assembly, and jurisdictional triage.
3. The most common administratively observable facts are documented requests, notice/timeline records, public-program rules or decisions, and hearing/grievance records.
4. The subset contains a smaller but especially important set of true doctrinal-gap cases where the plaintiff did not simply fail to plead; instead, doctrine itself is unstable, under-specified, or unusually demanding.
5. Those patterns map directly onto the note's two central arguments: administrative failure and verification mismatch. The losses repeatedly arise because public systems generate fragmented process facts but do not convert them into reliable, reviewable verification infrastructure.

### What is missing institutionally

Across the queue, the missing institutional functions are dominated by:

- `171` theory selection
- `146` fact development
- `93` document assembly
- `59` jurisdictional triage
- `25` administrative record building

That distribution matters. It suggests the public-process subset is not primarily failing because the underlying housing events are invisible in the world. The events are often visible. What is missing is the institutional machinery that converts them into legally usable records and claims.

### What is already administratively observable

The strongest fact-pattern counts are:

- `192` notice or timeline record
- `181` documented request
- `176` public-program rule or decision
- `65` hearing or grievance record
- `46` denial or no-response record
- `39` medical or provider letter

This is the clearest queue-level support for the note's verification-mismatch theory. Public systems are generating exactly the kinds of process artifacts that could support feature verification, but they are not being standardized, linked, surfaced, or made reviewable in a way that prevents litigation collapse.

## Strongest mechanisms

The completed outputs support four main mechanisms and one narrower doctrinal lane.

### 1. Translation failure remains the dominant mechanism

Translation is the single largest family in the queue (`62` cases), and the high-priority raw-text subset is also dominated by translation cases. The recurrent forms are familiar:

- request not adequately alleged
- disability nexus not adequately connected to the requested change
- complaint does not tie statutory elements to defendant-specific facts
- plaintiff experiences a public housing or municipal process failure but cannot convert it into a clean FHA theory

This mechanism remains especially important in pro se housing-authority and municipal cases. The earlier memo already showed that public-process cases are disproportionately early-stage and that pro se plaintiffs do dramatically worse, especially at MTD. The new queue sharpens that story: the missing work is often not discovery-scale fact creation, but translation of existing process materials into legally legible allegations.

Representative examples:

- `Elizabeth Fedynich; Nicole Fedynich v. Boulder Housing Partners` — high raw-text priority; housing-authority setting; request not adequately alleged.
- `Derry Sykes v. New York City Housing Authority` — high raw-text priority; disability nexus missing between emergency repair problems and accommodation theory.
- `Curtis K. Jackson, Sr. v. Chicago Housing Authority, et al.` — causal-link failure because the plaintiff targeted the wrong actor in a voucher/public-housing context.

This is the administrative-failure argument in miniature: the claimant often encounters a real process failure, but the public system does not supply an intake, record, or authority structure that helps translate the problem into the right legal form.

### 2. Jurisdictional and remedial misfit is the second major mechanism

The queue contains `33` procedural-gateway cases and `26` jurisdiction-or-standing mechanisms. That is too large to treat as noise. In public-process cases, plaintiffs often identify a real government-linked grievance but select a legally unavailable defendant, cause of action, or remedial path.

Representative examples:

- `Commissiong v. US Dept. Housing & Urban Development` — sovereign-immunity/private-right barrier against HUD.
- `Honkala et al v. U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development et al.` — standing failure because plaintiffs were not buyers or renters under the FHA.
- `Madeliene Y. Jeffreys v. Marcia L. Fudge, Secretary of U.S. Housing & Urban Development, et al.` — the grievance may support due-process analysis, but the FHA theory itself was unavailable.

This mechanism is especially note-relevant because it ties directly to administrative failure. When the agency with enforcement responsibility does not build usable compliance and review channels, litigants try to route grievances through whatever doctrinal door appears available. Many pick the wrong one. That is not just private pleading weakness; it is a structural symptom of a system that pushes disputes downstream into doctrinally poor substitutes.

### 3. Authority fragmentation and defendant mismatch remain central

The earlier memo identified institutional-authority fragmentation as the most note-relevant public-defendant mechanism. The completed queue confirms that judgment. The recurring pattern is that a documented request or housing problem exists, but the plaintiff cannot identify the official, office, contractor, architect, or entity that has the legally relevant authority.

The queue shows this in several ways:

- theory selection is the most common missing function
- document assembly and fact development remain high
- public-program rules and decisions are already present in `176` cases
- notice/timeline records are already present in `192` cases

The problem, in other words, is not absence of process. It is fractured process.

`Curtis K. Jackson, Sr. v. Chicago Housing Authority, et al.` is illustrative: the plaintiff had an observable access grievance, but the pleading failed because the allegations did not connect the denials to the correct defendant's conduct. Public systems multiply this mismatch by dispersing responsibility across authorities, landlords, inspectors, program administrators, and subcontractors.

That supports the note's argument that AFFH-style disability administration should reinforce authority-specific verification duties rather than rely on generic complaint pathways.

### 4. Record-heavy necessity standards create a public-law choke point

The queue also captures a distinct lane of cases where the dispute is developed, represented, and concrete, but the plaintiff still loses because doctrine demands unusually strict proof of necessity or technical fit.

The best examples come from municipal zoning/group-home cases:

- `Women's Elevated Sober Living L.L.C. v. City of Plano`
- `Constance Swanston, Women's Elevated Sober Living LLC, and Shannon Jones v. City of Plano, Texas`

Both are high doctrinal-gap cases. They suggest that even where the request is documented and the administrative process is clear, plaintiffs can still lose because disability-related therapeutic benefit does not satisfy a stricter judicial demand that the accommodation be "indispensable or essential." That is not an intake failure. It is a doctrinal filter.

This is a key refinement to the earlier memo. The public-process docket contains two overlapping populations:

1. a larger translation/record-assembly population, and
2. a smaller but more analytically valuable merits/doctrine population where the doctrine itself may be over-demanding or unstable.

### 5. Public wins identify the strongest doctrinal openings

The queue is not all loss cases. Several plaintiff-favorable cases are important precisely because they show where courts can be moved when process facts are stabilized.

Most important:

- `SoCal Recovery, LLC v. City of Costa Mesa` — high doctrinal-gap signal, but plaintiff-favorable. The Ninth Circuit allowed collective proof of actual disability through admissions criteria, policies, and procedures rather than insisting on individualized medical files.
- `New Beginning Sanctuary ... v. City of Springfield, Missouri` — reveals the importance of front-loading expert and technical support into the administrative request stage.

These cases matter for the note because they identify doctrinal lanes where better verification architecture can actually change outcomes. They show that courts are more receptive when disability, request, and administrative record are presented in structured rather than ad hoc form.

## Strongest doctrinal gaps

Only `11` queue cases are marked high doctrinal-gap, but they are disproportionately important because they help identify where reruns or raw-text review may produce doctrinally useful note material rather than just cleaner examples of routine pro se collapse.

### A. Necessity standard in municipal zoning/group-home cases

Priority cases:

- `Women's Elevated Sober Living L.L.C. v. City of Plano`
- `Constance Swanston ... v. City of Plano, Texas`
- `SoCal Recovery, LLC v. City of Costa Mesa`
- `New Beginning Sanctuary ... v. City of Springfield, Missouri`

Why this lane matters:

- It tests how courts define necessity in accommodation cases involving group homes, recovery housing, and density or occupancy limits.
- It surfaces a likely circuit-sensitive split between stricter and more functional approaches to proof.
- It connects directly to verification mismatch because these cases often rise or fall on whether operators can convert treatment realities, admissions criteria, and operational evidence into a record courts accept.

This is probably the strongest doctrinal lane for targeted follow-up because it combines public-process facts, a developed administrative record, and a question the note can use to show why better ex ante verification matters.

### B. FHA/private-right and standing gaps in challenges to HUD-linked process

Priority cases:

- `Commissiong v. US Dept. Housing & Urban Development`
- `Honkala et al v. U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development et al.`
- `Madeliene Y. Jeffreys v. Marcia L. Fudge, Secretary of U.S. Housing & Urban Development, et al.`

Why this lane matters:

- These cases show litigants trying to challenge public housing administration or HUD complaint outcomes but running into sovereign-immunity, standing, or private-right barriers.
- They connect directly to the note's administrative-failure thesis: when HUD does not supply usable administrative compliance infrastructure, litigants are pushed toward remedial dead ends.
- They also sharpen the note's reviewability/accountability sections by illustrating the limits of complaint-dependent enforcement.

### C. FCA/AFFH/public-certification doctrinal uncertainty

Priority case:

- `United States of America ex rel. James Dieter and Karen Schwenke v. City of Milwaukee ...`

Why it matters:

- The queue marks it high doctrinal-gap because it sits at the intersection of municipal housing administration, compliance certifications, and uncertain statutory hook/scienter/materiality requirements.
- In light of the synthesis memo's discussion of `ILC`, `Mei Ling`, and civil-rights-fraud enforcement, this case deserves attention as a possible contrast case: where certification-based public-law theories fail to become doctrinally usable without tighter statutory framing and record-building.

## What deserves most note attention now

### 1. Administrative failure is now more concrete

The queue gives stronger support for an important note-facing formulation: public disability cases frequently involve administratively observable events that are not institutionally converted into usable legal records. That is a narrower and stronger claim than simply saying public defendants are hostile or plaintiffs are under-resourced.

The most common observable facts are already process facts: requests, notices, decisions, hearings, denials. The system's failure lies in not making those facts legible, connected, and reviewable across fragmented institutions.

That dovetails with the synthesis memo's broader finding that HUD's disability enforcement problem is primarily administrative rather than conceptual. The queue shows that same pattern downstream in litigation behavior.

### 2. Verification mismatch is not abstract; it appears at case level

The queue is a case-level demonstration of the note's verification mismatch argument.

HUD and public housing systems often retain pieces of the relevant story:

- request logs
- transfer or voucher determinations
- hearing records
- notices
- recertification events
- municipal zoning or code decisions
- provider letters

But the public system does not produce the integrated, standardized verification package courts actually need. So plaintiffs fall back on complaint narratives that fail to connect request, disability nexus, official authority, and denial trail.

That is exactly the mismatch between pattern-oriented administration and feature-oriented disability enforcement. The missing information is often not demographic or spatial. It is request-level and decision-level.

### 3. The public-process subset strengthens the note's limiting principle

The queue also helps justify the note's preference for reinforcement rather than replication. The strongest fixes are not open-ended planning mandates. They are modest but consequential verification duties:

- standardized request logging
- authority mapping
- denial coding
- timeline capture
- administrative-record retention
- linkage of disability-related documentation to decision points

Those are targeted, administrable duties. They fit both the earlier public-process memo and the broader note architecture.

## Working conclusion

The disability-wave outputs do not displace the earlier public-defendant memo; they refine it. The earlier memo was right that public disability cases fail through process-heavy, authority-fragmented, documentation-dependent systems. The completed queue now shows more precisely how that happens.

The dominant pattern is still translation failure layered onto fragmented administration. But the completed queue also identifies a narrower set of higher-value doctrinal disputes, especially in municipal zoning and HUD-linked remedial cases, where raw-text review and targeted reruns could materially improve the note's doctrinal and institutional analysis.

The best note-facing synthesis is therefore:

- public/quasi-public disability cases repeatedly involve administratively observable housing-process facts;
- those facts are not converted into standardized, authority-specific, reviewable verification records;
- plaintiffs therefore lose through translation failure, remedial mismatch, and authority fragmentation;
- and in the smaller set of developed merits cases, doctrine itself sometimes imposes unusually strict necessity or fit standards that make ex ante verification even more important.

That is a strong bridge between the note's administrative-failure argument and its verification-mismatch argument. The queue shows that the disability problem is not just scarcity or hostility. It is a recurring failure to build and maintain the institutional record architecture that disability housing enforcement requires.
