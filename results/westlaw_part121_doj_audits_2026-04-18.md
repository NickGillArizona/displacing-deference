# Westlaw Part 121 + DOJ § 3604(f)(3) Audits — Synthesis Memo

**Date:** April 18, 2026
**Author:** Research assistant / note preparation
**Scope:**
 - (A) 8-PDF Part 121 set: `adv: 24 CFR s 121 And (disab OR handicap OR fair housing)` — complete Westlaw population citing 24 C.F.R. Part 121 in a fair-housing/disability context.
 - (B) 39-PDF DOJ set: `adv: TI(United States OR US) And (3604(f)(3) OR s 3604(f))` — complete Westlaw population of reported decisions where the United States is a captioned party in an FHA § 3604(f)(3) matter.

**Thesis linkage:** The note argues that HUD's 24 C.F.R. Part 121 disability-data-mandate (implementing 42 U.S.C. § 3614a) is dormant — under-promulgated, under-invoked, and under-enforced — producing a cascading enforcement gap. These two audits together test the thesis empirically from opposite directions: (A) every decision citing Part 121, and (B) every decision in which the United States itself litigated a § 3604(f)(3) disability claim. The **combined population** is the single strongest empirical window on whether Part 121 and § 3614a are operative in federal case law.

---

## HEADLINE FINDING

> **Across 47 federal decisions spanning 1978–2025 — the complete published population of (a) cases citing 24 C.F.R. Part 121 in any fair-housing/disability context AND (b) cases where the United States litigated FHA § 3604(f)(3) — the phrase "24 C.F.R. Part 121" appears substantively in exactly ONE opinion (ADAPT v. HUD, 1998 WL 113802, at *6 n.20, E.D. Pa. Mar. 12, 1998), and is there dismissed as "meritless." The companion statutory grant, 42 U.S.C. § 3614a, is cited exactly TWICE (Noble Homes, 173 F. Supp. 3d 568, 572 (N.D. Ohio 2016); Scott, 788 F. Supp. 1555, 1559 n.6 (D. Kan. 1992)), each time as a freestanding Chevron/deference preamble, never in support of enforcement.**

> **Three appearances in 47 opinions — plus one appellate affirmance (ADAPT v. HUD, 170 F.3d 381 (3d Cir. 1999)) that does not itself cite Part 121 — is the empirical signature of a dormant regulatory regime.**

### A.3 The ADAPT holding — the single on-point federal case

**ADAPT v. HUD**, 1998 WL 113802 (E.D. Pa. Mar. 12, 1998), aff'd, 170 F.3d 381 (3d Cir. 1999).

Plaintiffs — a coalition of disability advocacy organizations — alleged HUD's wholesale abdication of its FHAA disability-enforcement duty, including data-collection failure. The district court quoted the allegations verbatim:

> "Plaintiffs allege that HUD has received complaints of routine, nationwide non-compliance with the accessibility requirements of its Section 504 regulations, including a 1994 complaint by advocates for the disabled in response to which HUD officials allegedly acknowledged widespread compliance problems. Nonetheless, plaintiffs allege, HUD fails to (1) collect data on whether disabled persons benefit from its funding; (2) monitor grants before or after funds are spent to determine whether they are used to create accessible housing; (3) conduct prompt investigations of possible noncompliance; or (4) take enforcement action upon notice of noncompliance."
> — 1998 WL 113802, at *2.

The court then dispatched plaintiffs' Part 121 argument in a single footnote:

> "Plaintiffs' final argument, that the regulations implementing the FHAA's accessibility requirements, see 24 C.F.R. Part 121, provide law to apply to HUD's enforcement of its Section 504 regulations, is meritless."
> — Id. at *6 n.20.

The Third Circuit affirmed, 170 F.3d 381 (3d Cir. 1999), holding HUD enforcement discretion unreviewable under *Heckler v. Chaney*. The appellate opinion did not itself cite Part 121; it turned on § 3608(e)(5), 24 C.F.R. § 8.56(b), and general APA doctrine. (KeyCite yellow: *Turner v. Sec'y of HUD*, 449 F.3d 536 (3d Cir. 2006), distinguished on facts, not overruled.)

### A.4 What ADAPT proves — and does not prove — for the note

**Proves:**
 - Part 121 has not been judicially construed as imposing enforceable data-collection, monitoring, investigation, or enforcement duties on HUD.
 - Even disability-rights counsel explicitly arguing Part 121 as a source of "law to apply" for APA review failed at the district court, and did not even press the Part 121 theory on appeal.
 - The cascade logic is visible in the pleadings themselves: the 1994 complaint → HUD's acknowledgment of "widespread compliance problems" → HUD inaction → the lawsuit → the dismissal as unreviewable. That sequence is the data cascade in miniature.

**Does not prove:**
 - That Part 121 imposes *no* substantive duty — the court never reached the merits. The holding is justiciability, not merits.
 - That HUD has *never* complied with Part 121 — the case is about HUD's enforcement of Section 504 regulations *using* Part 121 data; the court reached no factual finding on whether Part 121 data is in fact collected.

### B.3 The Part 121 / § 3614a grid

**24 C.F.R. Part 121 citations in 39-case DOJ set:**
 - **Zero** substantive engagements. The only appearance of "Part 121" is in ADAPT v. HUD (File 27) fn. 20, already addressed above. ADAPT is captured here because HUD is a United States agency; it is not a DOJ enforcement action.

**42 U.S.C. § 3614a citations in 39-case DOJ set:**
 - **Two**, both purely jurisdictional / Chevron-deference preamble:
   1. *United States v. Noble Homes, Inc.*, 173 F. Supp. 3d 568, 572 (N.D. Ohio 2016): "Congress has delegated authority to HUD to administer the statute at issue pursuant to 42 U.S.C. § 3608(a) and to issue regulations regarding the statute pursuant to 42 U.S.C. § 3614a."
   2. *United States v. Scott*, 788 F. Supp. 1555, 1559 n.6 (D. Kan. 1992): "As an agency charged with administering the Fair Housing Act, pursuant to § 3614a, HUD's interpretation of the statute is entitled to considerable deference."

Neither opinion connects § 3614a to Part 121, to disability-data collection, or to the enforcement theory the DOJ actually pled.

**42 U.S.C. § 3614(a) pattern-or-practice citations:**
 - Present in virtually every DOJ case as the jurisdictional basis for the suit. Uniformly treated as a free-standing statutory grant of AG enforcement authority. Never linked to HUD administrative data, HUD referral, or Part 121 in any opinion surveyed. DOJ establishes the pattern through site inspections, architectural plans, and expert testimony — not through HUD-generated disability-data dashboards.

### B.5 Notable thesis-adjacent cases

**AIA v. HUD** (File 34), 74 F. Supp. 3d 30 (D.D.C. 2014), vacated and remanded, 646 F. App'x 7 (D.C. Cir. 2015) — struck down HUD's 2013 disparate-impact rule as ultra vires; subsequently displaced by *Tex. Dep't of Hous. & Cmty. Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, Inc.*, 576 U.S. 519 (2015). Relevant to the note as direct evidence that HUD rulemaking competence has been challenged on statutory-text grounds — the same kind of challenge a hypothetical Part 121 modernization would face. Red-flag KeyCite status; cite for the rulemaking-overreach narrative only, not for holding.

**Noble Homes** (File 12), 173 F. Supp. 3d 568 (N.D. Ohio 2016) — confirmed as the unique § 3614a citation in the DOJ enforcement corpus. The opinion cites § 3614a purely as delegation authority (at 572) and then discusses 24 C.F.R. § 100.200 et seq. (the accessibility regs) and the 1991 HUD Guidelines (56 Fed. Reg. 9472–9515) as the operative substantive standard. **The inferential point: when DOJ itself needs a regulatory anchor, it reaches for § 100.200 and the 1991 Guidelines, not Part 121.** That substitution is itself evidence of Part 121's dormancy as an operative enforcement tool.

**Scott** (File 32), 788 F. Supp. 1555 (D. Kan. 1992) — § 3614a citation is in a footnote supporting Chevron deference to HUD's interpretation; the case itself is a § 3604(f)(1)(B) covenant-interference suit, not a § 3604(f)(3) matter. KeyCite yellow (disagreed with on other grounds by *Mich. Prot. & Advocacy Serv. v. Babin*, 799 F. Supp. 695 (E.D. Mich. 1992)).

## PART C — CONSOLIDATED THESIS INTEGRATION

### C.1 What these two audits add to the note

1. **The denominator for Part 121 dormancy is ≤ 8** — the complete Westlaw population of cases citing Part 121 in a fair-housing/disability context. Of those 8, only 1 is a true engagement (ADAPT, fn. 20), and it is dismissive.

2. **The denominator for DOJ § 3604(f)(3) enforcement activity is ≈ 33** distinct reported matters across ~35 years. That is ~1 decision per year. Within that enforcement stream, **Part 121 is invoked zero times and § 3614a is invoked twice as throwaway boilerplate**.

3. **The combined n ≈ 47.** Three visible anchor citations (two § 3614a, one Part 121 fn. 20). Two actually-binding appellate decisions (ADAPT 3d Cir. 1999; Southern Management 4th Cir. 1992). The binding federal appellate authority on Part 121 is ADAPT — and ADAPT holds that Part 121 does not provide "law to apply" for APA review.

4. **DOJ substitutes HUD's 1991 Guidelines (56 Fed. Reg. 9472) and 24 C.F.R. § 100.200 et seq. for Part 121** when it needs a substantive regulatory anchor. This is the operational ratification of Part 121 dormancy: DOJ itself does not treat the data-mandate regulation as live law.

### C.2 Recommended integration into v89 drafts

1. **Branch III.C (Enforcement Gap)** — lead footnote should be the ADAPT *2 quotation of HUD's data-collection failure allegations, cited to both the district court opinion and the 3d Circuit affirmance. Include the fn. 20 "meritless" treatment as the parenthetical explaining why Part 121 cannot be judicially enforced.

2. **Branch I (Regulatory Landscape)** — add a short paragraph noting that in 39 DOJ § 3604(f)(3) enforcement actions, § 3614a is cited only twice and Part 121 zero times. ### C.4 What these audits do NOT establish

 - They do not count DOJ consent decrees and settlements that never generated a reported opinion. Those cases may or may not cite Part 121 in the complaint or settlement agreement; that would require a PACER or HUD FOIA pull. The "zero" finding is for reported opinion text, not DOJ practice writ large.
 - They do not show whether HUD has invoked Part 121 in its own administrative adjudications under § 3612. That would require a HUD-administrative-decision pull, which Westlaw does not fully index.
 - They do not foreclose the possibility that DOJ briefs (as opposed to opinions) invoked Part 121. The Westlaw full-text search did not catch such briefs in its party-caption filter; a PACER search of DOJ civil rights complaints would be needed to confirm.

All three of the above are natural extensions but do not change the core empirical finding that **the published federal case law is essentially silent on Part 121**.
