## Appendix I: AFFH Case Classification Methodology

### I.1 Corpus Construction

Seventy-one decisions were identified through CourtListener searches for "affirmatively further" AND "fair housing" in federal courts, supplemented by forward citation tracing from *Shannon v. HUD*, 436 F.2d 809 (3d Cir. 1970), *Hills v. Gautreaux*, 425 U.S. 284 (1976), and *NAACP v. Sec'y of HUD*, 817 F.2d 149 (1st Cir. 1987).

### I.2 Classification Protocol

Each decision was classified using LLM-assisted coding (Claude Haiku 4.5) with manual verification. Inclusion criteria: (1) the court addressed an AFFH obligation under § 3608(d) or (e) or a funding recipient's duty to affirmatively further fair housing; (2) the decision reached or discussed the substantive content of the AFFH mandate (excluding cases invoking AFFH only in background recitations). Twelve of the seventy-one decisions were false positives (not substantively addressing AFFH), leaving approximately fifty-nine actual AFFH cases. Each was classified by primary protected class based on the claim the court addressed.

### I.3 Results

Race was the primary protected class in 69% of cases. The corpus spans *Shannon* through *Anderson v. HUD*, 731 F. Supp. 3d 19 (D.D.C. 2024). Representative cases include *NAACP v. Sec'y of HUD*; *Thompson v. HUD*, 348 F. Supp. 2d 398 (D. Md. 2005); *Inclusive Cmtys. Project*, 860 F. Supp. 2d 312 (N.D. Tex. 2012); and the Westchester County decisions. All address racial segregation.

Disability is mentioned in twenty-eight of the seventy-one decisions (39%), but overwhelmingly in passing—as a preference category, background statutory recitation, or description of program purposes. *Comer v. Cisneros*, 37 F.3d 775 (2d Cir. 1994), and *Davis v. New York City Hous. Auth.*, 278 F.3d 64 (2d Cir. 2002), reference disability only as a preference category. Disability surfaces most substantively in *White v. City of Annapolis*, No. 1:20-cv-01268 (D. Md. 2020), and *Dieter v. City of Milwaukee*, No. 2:23-cv-00429 (E.D. Wis. 2023), but in both cases it appears as a parallel ADA or Rehabilitation Act claim alongside race-centered AFFH analysis—neither court applies the AFFH integration obligation to disability. No case in the corpus applies the AFFH integration obligation to disability—no court has used the desegregation or integration framework that defines AFFH doctrine to address disability accommodation, accessible-unit inventory, or § 3604(f)(3) compliance.
