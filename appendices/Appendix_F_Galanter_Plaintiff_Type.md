## Appendix F: Galanter Plaintiff-Type Analysis and Substance Abuse

### F.0 Galanter Analysis — RA Merits Win Rates by Plaintiff Type (Unified Dataset)

The Galanter repeat-player framework predicts that organizational and institutional plaintiffs will outperform individual litigants. The unified dataset confirms this prediction on RA merits claims:

| Plaintiff Type | RA Merits Claims | Plaintiff Wins | Win Rate |
|---------------|-----------------|----------------|----------|
| Individual tenant | 258 | 38 | 14.7% |
| Group home operator | 56 | 9 | 16.1% |
| Fair housing organization | 26 | 9 | 34.6% |
| Government | 12 | 2 | 16.7% |

Fair housing organizations achieve a 34.6% win rate — 2.35 times the individual tenant rate (14.7%). This gap is consistent with organizational plaintiffs' ability to select stronger cases, deploy experienced counsel, and frame claims within established doctrinal frameworks.

### F.1 Individual vs. Organizational Plaintiff Outcomes

The Substance Use win rate obscures a significant divergence between individual and organizational plaintiffs. Sober living and group home operators — particularly Oxford House — succeed at substantially higher rates than individual substance abuse claimants.

**Sober Living/Group Home/Zoning category:** n=166, P-Win Rate = 25.9%
**Substance Use disability category overall:** n=90, P-Win Rate = 16.1%

### F.2 The "Double Gatekeeping" Framework

Substance abuse claimants face two sequential legal hurdles:
1. **Status eligibility:** Establishing protected status under 42 U.S.C. § 3602(h)'s current-use exception — the claimant must demonstrate recovery or participation in a supervised recovery program.
2. **Accommodation merits:** Proving entitlement to the specific accommodation requested under standard § 3604(f)(3)(B) analysis.

No other disability subcategory faces this threshold status inquiry.

### F.3 "Flickering Protection"

The current-use exception creates a temporal dimension to disability status that does not exist for other protected classes. A person in recovery is protected; the same person experiencing relapse may lose protected status. This "flickering" quality complicates accommodation requests that require ongoing arrangements.
