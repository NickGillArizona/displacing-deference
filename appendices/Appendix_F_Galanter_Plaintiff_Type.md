## Appendix F: Galanter Plaintiff-Type Analysis — FHA Unified Database (Disability Cases)

### F.1 Theoretical Framework

Marc Galanter's repeat-player hypothesis predicts that organizational and institutional plaintiffs — "repeat players" who litigate frequently within a domain — will systematically outperform individual "one-shotters" due to advantages in case selection, litigation experience, doctrinal knowledge, and access to experienced counsel. This appendix tests that prediction against disability-only FHA cases drawn from a single source: the FHA Unified Database (n=1,720 disability cases with decided merits outcomes). All results are reported across three periods: Period 1 (1989-2009), Period 2 (2010-2014), and Period 3 (2015-2024).

Win rates are reported under two definitions. "Strict" counts only outright plaintiff victories on the merits (summary judgment for plaintiff, trial verdict for plaintiff, or equivalent). "Broad" adds outcomes favorable to plaintiffs that fall short of full victory, including favorable settlements achieved after a substantive judicial ruling and partial wins on key claims.

---

### F.2 Plaintiff-Type Win Rates — All Disability Cases (FHA Unified Database, n=1,720)

| Plaintiff Type | n (decided) | Strict Win Rate | Broad Win Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Individual tenant | 1,102 | 13.5% | 24.2% |
| Group home operator | 129 | 27.9% | 44.2% |
| Fair housing organization | 50 | 44.0% | 62.0% |
| Government | 22 | 54.5% | 86.4% |
| Other | 41 | 9.8% | 31.7% |

The data confirm Galanter's prediction with striking clarity. Government plaintiffs achieve a 54.5% strict win rate — more than four times the 13.5% rate for individual tenants. Fair housing organizations (44.0%) and group home operators (27.9%) occupy intermediate positions, each substantially outperforming individuals. The gradient is monotonic: as organizational capacity and litigation experience increase, so do win rates.

The broad win-rate column amplifies this pattern. Government plaintiffs secure favorable outcomes in 86.4% of decided cases, reflecting their capacity to extract favorable settlements even when full adjudication is unnecessary. Fair housing organizations reach 62.0%, and group home operators 44.2%. Individual tenants achieve broad wins in only 24.2% of cases — less than half the government rate and less than a third of the fair housing organization rate.

### F.3 Three-Period Trends by Plaintiff Type

#### F.3.1 Individual Tenants

| Period | n (decided) | Strict Win Rate | Broad Win Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| P1 (1989-2009) | 363 | 15.2% | 25.9% |
| P2 (2010-2014) | 97 | 2.1% | 12.4% |
| P3 (2015-2024) | 280 | 8.2% | 15.0% |

Individual tenants experience a dramatic collapse during Period 2, with the strict win rate falling from 15.2% to 2.1% — a decline of 86%. The broad rate drops comparably, from 25.9% to 12.4%. Period 3 shows only partial recovery: the strict rate climbs to 8.2% and the broad rate to 15.0%, but neither approaches Period 1 levels. The Period 2 trough aligns with the post-*Olmstead* doctrinal retrenchment and the increasing procedural complexity that disproportionately burdens unrepresented individual litigants.

#### F.3.2 Group Home Operators

| Period | n (decided) | Strict Win Rate | Broad Win Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| P1 (1989-2009) | 49 | 24.5% | 40.8% |
| P2 (2010-2014) | 11 | 36.4% | 54.5% |
| P3 (2015-2024) | 18 | 27.8% | 38.9% |

Group home operators display a strikingly different trajectory. Their strict win rate *increases* during Period 2 (from 24.5% to 36.4%), and the broad rate rises from 40.8% to 54.5%. Period 3 shows a modest decline but remains above Period 1 levels on the strict measure (27.8% vs. 24.5%). This counter-cyclical pattern is consistent with organizational plaintiffs' ability to weather doctrinal headwinds through experienced counsel, stronger case selection, and well-developed factual records — particularly in zoning and land-use disputes where group home operators have accumulated significant litigation expertise.

#### F.3.3 Fair Housing Organizations

| Period | n (decided) | Strict Win Rate | Broad Win Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| P1 (1989-2009) | 21 | 47.6% | 61.9% |
| P2 (2010-2014) | 4 | 75.0% | 75.0% |
| P3 (2015-2024) | 6 | 50.0% | 83.3% |

Fair housing organizations maintain the highest sustained win rates of any non-government plaintiff type across all three periods. Their strict rate never falls below 47.6%, and their broad rate reaches 83.3% in Period 3. The small sample sizes for Periods 2 and 3 (n=4 and n=6, respectively) warrant caution, but the consistency of high performance is notable. These organizations bring cases selectively, deploy attorneys with deep FHA expertise, and frequently develop the factual record through pre-litigation testing — all advantages unavailable to individual tenants.

### F.4 The Representation Gap: Plaintiff Type and Pro Se Status

| Plaintiff Type | % Pro Se | Pro Se Strict Win Rate | Represented Strict Win Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Individual tenant | 72.3% | 6.4% | 33.2% |
| Group home operator | 0.7% | — | 28.1% |
| Fair housing organization | 2.7% | — | 42.9% |
| Government | 0.0% | — | 54.5% |

The representation data reveal the structural mechanism underlying the Galanter gradient. Fully 72.3% of individual tenant plaintiffs proceed pro se, and their strict win rate is 6.4% — roughly one-fifth the rate achieved by represented individual tenants (33.2%). The gap of 26.8 percentage points constitutes the single largest explanatory variable in the plaintiff-type analysis. Represented individual tenants (33.2% strict) perform comparably to represented group home operators (28.1%) and approach the represented fair housing organization rate (42.9%), suggesting that much of the apparent organizational advantage is in fact a representation advantage.

The near-total absence of pro se litigants among organizational plaintiff types (0.7% for group home operators, 2.7% for fair housing organizations, 0.0% for government) confirms that the Galanter effect operates primarily through the channel of counsel quality and availability rather than through some intrinsic organizational litigation advantage.

### F.5 Implications for Disability Rights Enforcement

Three conclusions emerge from the plaintiff-type analysis:

1. **The access-to-justice crisis dominates outcomes.** The 72.3% pro se rate among individual tenants, combined with a 6.4% strict win rate for unrepresented plaintiffs, means that the modal disability discrimination plaintiff enters the federal courts without counsel and exits without relief. The system fails most acutely for the population it is designed to protect.

2. **Organizational plaintiffs are partially insulated from doctrinal retrenchment.** While individual tenant win rates collapsed during Period 2, group home operators and fair housing organizations maintained or improved their performance. This divergence suggests that procedural complexity and heightened evidentiary standards imposed during the post-2010 period function as gatekeeping mechanisms that organizational resources can overcome but individual litigants cannot.

3. **Representation is the primary mechanism, not plaintiff type per se.** When representation is held constant, the gap between individual and organizational plaintiffs narrows considerably. The policy implication is direct: expanding access to competent FHA counsel for individual disability discrimination plaintiffs would likely produce larger improvements in outcomes than any doctrinal reform.
