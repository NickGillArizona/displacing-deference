### G.6 Interpretation and Caveats

Circuit-level variation reveals a pronounced geographic dimension to disability FHA enforcement. Key patterns:

1. **Overall win rates.** The D.C. Circuit (38.2% broad) and the 7th Circuit (36.8%) are the most favorable to disability plaintiffs; the 4th Circuit (17.3%) and the 2nd Circuit (23.2%) are the least favorable. The strict-to-broad gap is widest in the 4th Circuit (5.1% strict vs. 17.3% broad), indicating that a disproportionate share of favorable outcomes there depend on procedural concessions rather than merits rulings.

2. **MTD gatekeeping.** The 1st Circuit shows the highest MTD survival rate (45.8% broad), while the 2nd Circuit (14.5%) and 4th Circuit (17.2%) are the most hostile at the pleading stage. The 2nd Circuit's low rate is substantially explained by the S.D.N.Y. pro se concentration effect.

3. **Binary pre/post decline.** The decline is not uniform across circuits. The D.C. Circuit (-37.0 pp), the 10th Circuit (-23.7 pp), and the 2nd Circuit (-21.2 pp) experienced the steepest drops; the 6th Circuit (-2.4 pp), 7th Circuit (-2.9 pp), and 3rd Circuit (+4.8 pp) showed relative resilience or improvement.

4. **Three-period MTD trends.** When Period 1 MTD rates are compared to the combined Period 2 and Period 3 rates, the 11th Circuit shows the sharpest collapse (-38.1 pp, falling to 0.0%), followed by the 5th Circuit (-26.0 pp). The 3rd Circuit (+0.6 pp) and 10th Circuit (-1.0 pp) are essentially flat. The 4th Circuit is the sole circuit to show improvement (+7.7 pp), though from a very low Period 1 baseline (4.8%).

5. **Interactive process engagement.** The 1st Circuit discusses the interactive process at a far higher rate (27.3%) than any other circuit, more than five times the rate of the 3rd Circuit (3.2%). This variation likely reflects doctrinal differences in how circuits incorporate the interactive process into reasonable-accommodation analysis under Section 3604(f)(3)(B).

**Caveat:** Circuit-level sample sizes are modest (ranging from 34 to 289 decided cases per circuit), and individual circuit rates should be interpreted as descriptive observations warranting further study, not as established differences in circuit doctrine. Circuit assignments were derived from district court names; exact mappings should be verified against filing records.
