## Appendix J: Safe Harbor Operational Detail

This appendix provides the operational mechanics underlying the three-element safe harbor framework proposed in the Note's Part IV.C. The Note's body text presents the safe harbor at the conceptual level; this appendix develops the implementation detail — worked examples, edge-case protocols, regulatory architecture, and enforcement mechanics — that would inform a notice-and-comment rulemaking proceeding. The three-element structure tracks the Note's constitutional analysis: each element stays on the compliance-verification side of the ministerial/substantive line drawn throughout Part III.

---

### J.1. Worked Example: Qualifying vs. Disqualifying Developments

Consider a hypothetical 100-unit mixed-income development in which a specified percentage of units meet federal accessibility standards, located on a bus line, with individual leases, no mandatory programming, a service-coordination agreement with a community mental health center one-quarter mile away, and a grocery store and employment center within one mile. Under Element 2's community-integration criteria, this development satisfies transit access, service coordination, community amenities, and absence of institutional indicia. Residents choose whether to use off-site services; no staff controls entry or exit; each unit has a standard residential lease.

Contrast this with the congregate care facilities *Disability Advocates, Inc. v. Paterson* condemned: 80-95% disability populations, mandatory congregate meals, staff-controlled daily schedules, institutional intake procedures, and isolation from community life.(Note 176) The functional difference — individual autonomy, community integration, voluntary services — is what *Olmstead*'s "most integrated setting" inquiry targets, not the numerical percentage of disabled residents. The SAMHSA Fidelity Scale rates the 26-50% range at its second-highest integration score precisely because it preserves the mixed-population environment that prevents institutional dynamics.(Note 162) Element 2's criteria do the heavy lifting: a development that fails the community-integration criteria receives no safe-harbor protection regardless of its accessibility threshold.

### J.2. Element 1: Accessibility Threshold — Operational Detail

#### J.2.1. Cost Analysis

The economic case for ex-ante compliance is straightforward. Incorporating full accessibility (UFAS or ICC A117.1 at the "Accessible" or "Type A" unit level) during new construction adds less than one percent to total construction cost — a marginal premium that disappears at scale. Retrofitting existing Type B units to UFAS/Type A standards, by contrast, costs $26,000 to $80,000 per unit depending on structural constraints, unit configuration, and local labor markets. The 47% design-and-construction noncompliance rate documented in Part I.D means the housing stock is accumulating retrofit liabilities that ex-ante compliance would have avoided entirely. The safe harbor's accessibility threshold is designed to shift the incentive structure from costly after-the-fact remediation to marginal-cost compliance at the design stage.

#### J.2.2. Population Threshold Context

The Note's body text does not specify a numerical population threshold, deferring instead to notice-and-comment rulemaking calibrated to documented need. This appendix provides the regulatory context that would inform that rulemaking.

Independent convergence across multiple state programs supports a threshold in the range of 25-50% disability set-aside within mixed-income developments. Section J.6 surveys New York (50% ESSHI/SHOP ceiling), California (49% NPLH cap with layering flexibility), Oregon (no explicit ceiling but 20% Type A accessibility requirement), and SAMHSA's Permanent Supportive Housing Fidelity Scale (26-50% range receives second-highest integration score). The convergence across independent programs — each designed by different state agencies with different political constituencies and housing markets — provides evidence that a threshold in this range falls within the *State Farm* zone of reasonableness.(Note 160) Critically, a higher disability set-aside makes Element 2's community-integration criteria more load-bearing: qualitative safeguards against institutional character substitute for a lower numerical line.

#### J.2.3. Section 811 Reconciliation

The proposed safe harbor must be reconciled with Section 811's existing statutory framework. Section 8013(d)(4)(A)(iv) of the Cranston-Gonzalez National Affordable Housing Act caps 811 Project Rental Assistance (PRA) units at 25% of total units in any single development. This cap is a *funding condition* — it limits the proportion of units supported by 811 PRA subsidy, not the proportion of units occupied by disabled tenants or designed to federal accessibility standards.

The distinction is critical. A developer can satisfy the safe harbor's accessibility threshold through non-811 capital stacking that is already standard practice across multiple state programs. New York's ESSHI layered 811 PRA with state housing trust fund allocations and LIHTC equity to produce developments at 50% disability set-aside without exceeding 811's 25% per-project cap. California's NPLH operates similarly, layering NPLH-assisted units (capped at 49%) with TCAC and other state allocations. In each case, the funding above the 811 threshold uses capital sources Congress did not subject to the § 8013(d)(4)(A)(iv) limitation.

The safe harbor therefore does not conflict with Section 811: 811 PRA units count toward the accessibility threshold, but the threshold itself is financed through diversified capital stacking rather than through any single federal subsidy program. The regulatory framework should make this explicit to prevent grantees from treating the 811 cap as a ceiling on disability occupancy generally.

### J.3. Element 2: Community-Integration Criteria — Operational Detail

Element 2 operates as a negative institutional-character screen: it assesses whether a development functions like an institution rather than whether it achieves demographic integration targets. The criteria are objectively verifiable and draw on the CMS HCBS Settings Rule's distinction between community-based and institutional settings.

**Table J-1: Element 2 Criteria Applied**

| Criterion | Qualifying Development | Disqualifying Development |
|---|---|---|
| Transit access | On bus line (fixed-route) or within ADA-compliant demand-response service area | Isolated rural road; no demand-response service |
| Service coordination | Written agreement (annual renewal) with off-site CMHC or ILC | No off-site service agreement; on-site staff only |
| Community participation | Residents use employment center, recreation, or education programs | No documented non-residential participation |
| Institutional indicia | Individual leases; voluntary services; no staff-controlled entry | Mandatory congregate meals; staff-controlled schedules; institutional intake |
| Safe-harbor eligibility | **Presumption of non-concentration** | **No safe-harbor protection** |

A development meeting the Element 1 accessibility threshold but exhibiting institutional character receives no safe-harbor protection regardless of its compliance metrics.

#### J.3.1. Edge Case: Rural Non-Entitlement Communities

An edge case tests the framework's limits: a rural non-entitlement community with no fixed-route transit and limited community amenities. Element 2's alternative — location within an ADA-compliant demand-response service area — addresses the transit criterion, but the community-amenities requirement may exclude genuinely isolated rural developments. This limitation is deliberate: the safe harbor is designed for community-integrated housing, and developments that cannot satisfy community-integration criteria require individual *Olmstead* analysis rather than a categorical presumption.

The Availability Audit (Element 3) provides the complementary safeguard: if no accessible units exist in higher-opportunity tracts within the PHA's service area, the safe harbor applies with heightened monitoring; if accessible alternatives exist, the safe harbor is unavailable. Where accessible units exist but face multi-year waitlists, the Audit treats waitlisted units as functionally unavailable — consistent with the principle that a right without a remedy is no right at all.

**Rural/Tribal Adaptations:**
- *Transit*: Demand-response service area satisfies the transit requirement where fixed-route transit is absent
- *Service coordination*: Documented telehealth-coordination agreement with a qualified provider (with on-site broadband) satisfies the service-coordination requirement where no community mental health center or independent living center exists within the service area

This framework produces three scenarios for rural developments:

1. **Rural development meeting all Element 2 criteria** (e.g., within ADA demand-response area, service-coordination agreement with nearest ILC, documented community participation): Safe harbor applies on standard terms.

2. **Rural development meeting Element 2 except transit** (e.g., no fixed-route transit and outside demand-response area, but meeting all other criteria): Safe harbor unavailable; development proceeds under individual *Olmstead* analysis. The Availability Audit may independently support the siting if no accessible alternatives exist in higher-opportunity tracts.

3. **Rural development in a community with no accessible housing stock** (e.g., tribal or frontier community where the Availability Audit reveals zero comparable accessible units in any tract): Safe harbor applies with heightened monitoring — annual recertification of community-integration criteria and biennial REAC accessibility inspection.

### J.4. Element 3: Availability Audit — Mechanics

#### J.4.1. Query Protocol

The Audit mechanism operates as follows: the developer queries the PHA's accessible-housing inventory for comparable units in higher-opportunity tracts; if none exist, the safe harbor applies; if alternatives exist, it is revoked.(Note 179)

During the interim phase (before national inventory data exists), developers seeking the safe harbor certify, subject to False Claims Act liability (31 U.S.C. § 3729), that a standardized search of PHA inventories, state LIHTC databases, accessible-housing registries, and HUD mapping tools revealed no comparable accessible units in higher-opportunity tracts. If all queries return negative results, the safe harbor applies under a rebuttable presumption; if comparable units are identified, the safe harbor is revoked. This interim mechanism is imperfect — it shifts the burden to developers rather than relying on a comprehensive federal inventory — but it provides a meaningful anti-concentration check from day one, and FCA liability creates enforcement teeth that self-certification alone lacks.

#### J.4.2. Regulatory Architecture

The Audit's architecture draws on 24 C.F.R. § 983.55(e)(3), which permits PBV siting in minority-concentrated areas only if comparable opportunities exist elsewhere, assessed through a seven-factor framework including an anti-circumvention clause.(Note 177) The Audit extends this standard from *racial* concentration in *PBV* housing to *disability* concentration in *safe harbor* housing.(Note 178)

The seven-factor framework adapted for disability concentration requires assessment of:

1. Whether comparable accessible units exist in non-concentrated tracts within the PHA service area
2. The occupancy rate and waitlist length for identified comparable units
3. Whether the proposed development serves a population (e.g., persons with specific mobility or sensory impairments) for which existing accessible stock is inadequate
4. The adequacy of public transportation between the proposed site and employment, healthcare, and community services
5. Whether the proposed site offers proximity to disability-specific services (e.g., community mental health centers, independent living centers) unavailable in alternative locations
6. Whether the developer has undertaken good-faith efforts to identify alternative sites in non-concentrated areas
7. Whether the accessible-unit scarcity cited in support of the siting is itself a product of design-and-construction noncompliance that the anti-circumvention clause is designed to prevent

#### J.4.3. Anti-Circumvention Clause

The anti-circumvention clause prevents developers from citing accessible-unit scarcity that design-and-construction noncompliance itself produced.(Note 180) (Note 181) A developer cannot benefit from the safe harbor by locating in a high-concentration area and pointing to the absence of accessible units elsewhere when that absence results from the very noncompliance the FHA's design-and-construction requirements were enacted to prevent. The developer's burden of demonstrating scarcity parallels the market study already standard in LIHTC underwriting.(Note 153)

Where the accessible-unit deficit in higher-opportunity tracts results from documented § 3604(f)(3)(C) noncompliance by the same developer or its affiliates, the Audit treats those tracts as having the accessible inventory they would have had under compliance. This closes the most obvious manipulation pathway: a developer with a portfolio of noncompliant buildings in higher-opportunity areas cannot cite the resulting scarcity to justify concentrated siting elsewhere.

#### J.4.4. Phased Implementation

| Phase | Data Source | Audit Mechanism | Anti-Concentration Check |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Immediate** (existing data) | PHA records, state LIHTC databases, accessible-housing registries, HUD mapping tools | Developer self-certification under FCA liability | Rebuttable presumption if all queries negative |
| **Medium-term** (PHA registries) | PHA-compiled accessible-unit registries (modeled on MassAccess) | PHA-verified inventory query | Direct inventory comparison; waitlist-adjusted |
| **Long-term** (national inventory) | National accessible-housing inventory with cross-tabulated race-disability data | Full alternatives analysis | Comprehensive tract-level comparison with anti-circumvention review |

#### J.4.5. False Claims Act Enforcement and *Escobar* Materiality

Self-certification of accessibility compliance as a condition of federal housing funds creates a direct *qui tam* predicate under 31 U.S.C. § 3730(b). Any person with knowledge of a materially false certification — a disability rights organization, building inspector, former employee, or REAC audit reviewer — may bring a relator action without requiring individual accommodation denial or particularized injury. This addresses the standing gap identified in the Note's Part I.D: the disparity between 47% documented noncompliance and 0.8% complaint rates reflects standing barriers that individualized litigation cannot overcome. The FCA pathway bypasses those barriers entirely.

The materiality standard under *Universal Health Services, Inc. v. United States ex rel. Escobar*, 579 U.S. 176, 192-93 (2016), is readily satisfied where the certification concerns: (1) a physical building characteristic (accessible-unit count and design compliance), (2) that is objectively verifiable through inspection, and (3) that is directly tied to federal funding eligibility through AFFH certification. *Escobar* requires that the false statement be "material to the Government's payment decision" — a standard the proposed framework satisfies by making accessibility compliance an express condition of AFFH certification.

Critically, self-certification under the proposed regulatory framework resets the materiality baseline. Under the prior regime of known-but-tolerated noncompliance — where HUD acknowledged systemic design-and-construction violations but took no enforcement action — a developer could argue that the government's continued funding despite known noncompliance negated materiality. The proposed framework eliminates this defense by making compliance certification an affirmative condition of funding rather than a background expectation. A developer who certifies compliance and is later found noncompliant cannot argue that HUD's prior tolerance of noncompliance demonstrates immateriality, because the new regulatory framework expressly conditions funding on the certification's accuracy.

The FCA pathway is the structural mechanism that makes the disability model self-executing: it creates a private enforcement channel that operates through private relators without HUD initiative, independent of administrative continuity, and powered by the financial incentives (15-30% of recovered damages) that attract experienced *qui tam* counsel. The integration model required a sympathetic administration at every stage of enforcement; the FCA pathway requires one only at creation of the certification requirement.

### J.5. Self-Tightening Mechanism

The Availability Audit operates as a structural anti-concentration safeguard that self-tightens as accessible-unit inventory grows. As the national accessible-unit inventory expands through Tier 1 data collection and Tier 2 compliance verification, the number of tracts with documented accessible units increases. This makes it progressively harder for developers to demonstrate the absence of comparable accessible alternatives in higher-opportunity tracts — the threshold for invoking the safe harbor rises automatically without any regulatory amendment. The strongest version of the integration-abandonment objection (Part IV.D) becomes the model's own enforcement mechanism: the more accessible units the disability model produces, the harder it becomes to site new accessible housing in concentrated areas.

### J.6. Multi-Jurisdiction Population Threshold Survey

The following survey provides the regulatory context for notice-and-comment rulemaking on the accessibility threshold percentage. Independent convergence across multiple state programs supports a threshold in the 25-50% range:

**New York**: The Empire State Supportive Housing Initiative (ESSHI) states that developments are "considered integrated, provided that no more than 50% of the total units are reserved for individuals with disabilities."(Note 163) New York's Supportive Housing Opportunity Program (SHOP) operates under the same ceiling.

**California**: The No Place Like Home (NPLH) program caps NPLH-assisted units at 49% of total project units but permits layered funding from other sources (including TCAC) to produce higher disability concentrations within the same development.(Note 164) This structure effectively allows developments to exceed 49% disability occupancy through non-NPLH funding while maintaining the program's facial integration commitment.

**Oregon**: Oregon's permanent supportive housing standards impose no explicit upper limit on the proportion of PSH units within a mixed development while requiring voluntary community-based services, comprehensive tenancy support at a minimum 1:15 staffing ratio, and a minimum 20% Type A accessible units.(Note 165) Projects range from five to seventy-five PSH units.

**SAMHSA Fidelity Scale**: The Permanent Supportive Housing Fidelity Scale scores integration on a 4-point scale: 4 (highest) for 0-25% disability set-aside, 3 for 26-50%, 2 for 51-75%, and 1 for 76-100%.(Note 162) The 26-50% range thus receives the Scale's second-highest integration rating, above majority-disability and near-total-disability settings.

The convergence across these independent programs — each designed by different state agencies with different political constituencies and housing markets — provides evidence that a threshold in this range falls within the *State Farm* zone of reasonableness.(Note 160)
