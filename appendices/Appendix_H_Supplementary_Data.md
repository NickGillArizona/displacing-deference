Appeal outcomes deteriorated markedly from P1 (35.7% broad) to P3 (18.2% broad).*

### H.7 Delay-as-Denial Analysis

**Prevalence:** 70 cases invoke delay-as-denial (4.1% of all disability cases in the FHA Unified Database).

| DaD Invoked | Decided n | PW Strict | Broad Rate |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Yes | 64 | 39.1% | 65.6% |
| No | 1,280 | 15.4% | 27.0% |

*Delay-as-denial recognition is associated with dramatically higher plaintiff success: strict win rates are 2.5x higher and broad rates are 2.4x higher when courts recognize unreasonable delay as a constructive denial of accommodation.*

### H.8 Interactive Process and Delay-as-Denial Co-Occurrence

Of 189 cases in which courts discuss the interactive process, delay-as-denial is also invoked in a subset. The combined IP + DaD co-occurrence reflects overlapping doctrinal frameworks: courts that engage the interactive process framework are more likely to scrutinize temporal dimensions of the accommodation request, creating a pathway to delay-as-denial recognition.

### H.9 *Loper Bright* Citation Analysis

Total cases citing *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*: **2** (both in Period 3). The near-absence of *Loper Bright* citations in disability FHA litigation to date reflects the decision's nascency rather than irrelevance; as discussed in Part IV, the removal of *Chevron* deference has significant implications for HUD regulatory interpretations underlying reasonable accommodation and modification standards.

### H.10 Pro Se Outcomes by Defendant Type (All Disability Decided)

| Defendant Type | Pro Se PW Strict (n) | Represented PW Strict (n) |
|----------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Property Management | 6.0% (10/167) | 45.2% (28/62) |
| HOA | 7.0% (4/57) | 39.0% (30/77) |
| Private Landlord | 10.8% (27/250) | 41.2% (47/114) |
| Housing Authority | 4.9% (7/143) | 16.7% (7/42) |
| Municipality | 3.3% (2/61) | 23.3% (35/150) |

*The representation gap is stark across all defendant types. Pro se plaintiffs fare worst against municipalities (3.3%) and housing authorities (4.9%), precisely the institutional defendants most likely to employ experienced counsel. Even against private landlords -- the smallest-scale defendants -- pro se win rates (10.8%) are less than one-third of represented plaintiff rates (41.2%). The HOA disparity (7.0% vs. 39.0%) is particularly notable given that HOA disputes often involve well-documented accommodation requests where the factual record should favor plaintiffs.*
